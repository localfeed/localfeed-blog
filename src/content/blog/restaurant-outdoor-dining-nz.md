A well-designed outdoor dining space is one of the most valuable physical assets a NZ restaurant can have. In the right seasons and climates, it can add 20–40% to your seating capacity with minimal ongoing cost. It photographs well for social media, creates a desirable atmosphere, and signals to passing foot traffic that something good is happening.

Here is how to make it work properly.

## The Design Questions

**Weatherproofing**: In most NZ cities, outdoor dining without weather protection is usable for only four to five months of the year. Retractable awnings, parasols, or a fixed pergola structure extend the season. In Wellington, wind protection is as important as rain protection. In Auckland, rain shelter matters; wind is less consistently a problem.

**Heating**: Patio heaters extend the viable season by four to eight weeks at either end of summer. The cost of running patio heaters is meaningful but usually justified by the additional covers they enable.

**Acoustic separation**: Outdoor diners in a street-facing space are subject to traffic noise. Natural or built screening (hedges, lattice, planters) can significantly improve the perceived atmosphere without major construction.

**Licensing**: Depending on your local council and your liquor licence conditions, serving alcohol in an outdoor space may require specific licence conditions. Check with your local council and your licensing attorney before serving alcohol outdoors.

## Staffing Outdoor Sections

The outdoor section is not a satellite — it is part of your floor. It needs dedicated coverage during service, clear communication with the kitchen, and the same service standard as the indoor section.

Common failures:
- Outdoor guests waiting significantly longer for service than indoor
- Difficulty flagging down staff from an outdoor table
- Outdoor menus that are a subset of the indoor menu without explanation

Avoid these: roster outdoor section coverage explicitly, not as an overflow for your indoor floor.

## Weather Contingency

For bookings that might be affected by weather, have a policy and communicate it in advance:

"We will be in touch the morning of your booking to confirm your preference for outdoor or indoor seating if the weather is uncertain."

This prevents the uncomfortable situation where a group arrived expecting an outdoor table and the weather made it unusable. One proactive message the morning of the booking handles it.

## Marketing the Outdoor Space

Outdoor dining photographs better than indoor in good light. Use it deliberately:

- Summer Instagram content featuring the outdoor space
- Google Business Profile photos of the outdoor section during good weather
- Specific mention of outdoor seating in your Google profile and website

During summer, "outdoor dining Ponsonby" or "alfresco restaurant Wellington" are searchable terms with real intent. Appearing in those results requires having the right keywords in your Google profile.

## The Off-Season Pivot

What happens to your outdoor space in winter? The worst outcome is an empty, disused area that signals low activity to passing foot traffic. Consider:

- Wrapping the space for winter with temporary weather protection
- Using it for storage that is not visible from the street
- Converting it seasonally to a different use — a takeaway window, a pick-up point, a plant display that looks intentional

The outdoor space in winter should not communicate "this venue is at reduced capacity." It should look like a deliberate winter configuration.
