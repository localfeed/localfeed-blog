The technology stack available to NZ restaurant operators has expanded enormously. POS systems, booking platforms, delivery integrations, kitchen display systems, table ordering, CRM, payroll software, inventory management, social media scheduling — every category has multiple vendors, all claiming they will transform your business.

Most of them will not. Some of them will. Here is the honest assessment.

## The Technology That Actually Moves the Needle

### POS System

This is the most important technology decision a NZ restaurant makes. A good POS system provides accurate sales data, integrates with your accounting software, handles split bills and table management, and gives you the cost-of-sales visibility that drives financial management.

The leading options in NZ in 2026: Lightspeed, Kounta (now Lightspeed), Square, and for larger operations, Toast and Revel. The right choice depends on your volume, your reporting needs, and your accounting software.

What to insist on: real-time sales data, product-level sales mix reporting (so you know which items actually sell), and clean integration with Xero or MYOB.

What does not matter as much: the interface aesthetics, the number of integrations available in theory.

### Booking System / Platform

A booking system that captures customer contact data is commercially more valuable than one that does not. The booking data — who came, when, how many, their contact details — is the foundation of a direct customer relationship.

Evaluate booking platforms on: do I own the customer data? What is the real cost per booking (fee plus margin impact of any mandatory discounting)? Does it send reminders that reduce no-shows?

[LocalFeed](https://localfeed.app) provides NZ venues with full customer contact data from every booking, charges a flat $10/week (not per-booking commission), and requires no mandatory discounting.

### Payroll / HR Software

Employing staff in New Zealand has specific compliance requirements. Payroll software that handles NZ leave entitlements, public holiday calculations, and Kiwisaver contributions correctly is essential. Doing payroll manually with NZ leave entitlements calculated by hand is a compliance risk.

Recommended: Smartly, Tanda (which also handles rostering and time and attendance), or Employment Hero. Integration with your POS for tip calculations and hour tracking is valuable.

### Inventory / Stock Management

For venues doing significant volume or with complex menus, inventory management software that integrates with your POS to track stock usage against sales can reduce food waste and highlight variance (the gap between theoretical and actual cost, which often indicates wastage or theft).

For smaller venues, a disciplined weekly manual stock count tracked in a spreadsheet achieves most of the same outcome at zero additional cost.

## The Technology That Creates Complexity Without Return

### Table Ordering Apps

QR-code-based table ordering has been introduced and removed by numerous NZ venues since 2020. The customer satisfaction data is mixed. Many NZ diners prefer human interaction for ordering. The technology works well for specific contexts (quick-service, high-volume casual) and poorly for others (relationship-driven dining, complex menus, upsell-dependent service).

Before investing: does table ordering actually serve your diner and your business model, or does it reduce the service quality that brings your customers back?

### Delivery Integration Middleware

If you are on Uber Eats, DoorDash, and your own delivery channel simultaneously, middleware that manages all orders through one interface reduces confusion. But the underlying question — whether third-party delivery is commercially worthwhile for your venue — should come first. At 25–35% commission, delivery platforms have very different economics than dine-in.

### AI Scheduling and Demand Prediction

Several platforms now offer AI-powered roster scheduling and demand prediction. For most NZ restaurants doing under $3M in annual revenue, the cost and complexity of these systems is not justified by the marginal improvement over an experienced operator's intuition with decent POS data.

## The Technology Evaluation Framework

For every technology investment, ask three questions:

**1. What does it replace?** If it replaces a manual process, estimate the time saved. If it replaces nothing (it is purely additive), be sceptical.

**2. What is the actual all-in cost?** Monthly subscription plus set-up plus training plus ongoing maintenance plus any lock-in costs. Not just the headline subscription price.

**3. What would the business look like without it in 12 months?** If the honest answer is "roughly the same," the technology is not solving a real problem.

Most NZ restaurants need four categories of technology functioning well: a solid POS, a booking system that captures customer data, payroll compliance software, and accounting. Everything else is optional.
