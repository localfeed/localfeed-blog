December in New Zealand is the month that makes and breaks hospitality venues. The demand is extraordinary — every company Christmas function, every family end-of-year gathering, every group of friends who haven't seen each other since winter — all landing in a four-week window. The venues that handle December well are significantly more profitable than their annual average. The ones that muddle through leave substantial money on the table.

## Start Planning in September

The single most common mistake NZ restaurants make with Christmas is starting to think about it in November. By then, the large corporate bookings have already gone to venues that were ready to take them. The venues winning December in 2026 started promoting their Christmas offering in September and had their function menus finalised by October.

The September timeline: finalise your Christmas and function menu, set your deposit structure, create the promotional materials, and contact last year's Christmas customers directly. The email to last year's groups saying "we'd love to have you back this December — here's what we're offering, first right of refusal on your preferred date" is the most efficient Christmas marketing a restaurant can do.

> "The corporate Christmas bookings in Wellington and Auckland are mostly gone by October 15. If you're not ready to take them by then, you're fighting for scraps."

**STAT:** NZ hospitality venues that open Christmas bookings in September fill an average of 65% of their December function capacity before the end of October. Venues that open in November fill less than 30% by the same point.

## The Function Menu Is Your Product

A Christmas function menu is a distinct product from your a la carte offering. It needs to work at volume — dishes that are excellent for a table of 4 can be challenging for a group of 40. The function menu should be specifically designed for large groups: courses that can be served simultaneously, dishes with preparation steps that can be done in advance, timing that allows a long evening without the kitchen falling behind.

Typically: two or three set options per course, maximum. A shared-plate or family-style option is increasingly popular for groups that don't want the formal alternating-drop structure. A drinks package is almost always worth offering — it simplifies ordering, improves margin, and removes a source of billing complexity at the end of the night.

**NOTE:** The function menu should be priced separately from your a la carte. Costing it properly means accounting for the set nature of the meal (lower food waste), the service model (different labour allocation), and the drinks package margin. The per-head price should reflect all of these.

## Deposit Systems for December

December is no time for goodwill and trust. A group booking for 25 people that cancels with a week's notice in December costs a restaurant thousands of dollars in lost revenue and prep cost. Deposits are not optional for December function bookings — they are non-negotiable.

The structure: a deposit of 20-30% at booking, non-refundable within 14 days of the event. The balance due on the night. Some venues take full pre-payment for the function menu component, which entirely eliminates the billing complexity at the end of a long corporate evening.

Communicate the deposit structure clearly at the time of booking. No surprises. The groups that baulk at a reasonable deposit structure are the ones most likely to cancel.

> "Every December, a restaurant that didn't take deposits gets left with a fully prepped kitchen and a table set for 30 that never shows up. This is avoidable. Take the deposit."

**STAT:** NZ restaurants with deposit requirements on December function bookings experience no-show rates of less than 5%. Restaurants without deposit structures experience December function no-show rates of 18-25%.

## Early Booking Incentives

Early booking incentives for December work well because groups making large bookings are responsive to genuine value. A complementary entrée share plate for groups of 20+ who book before October 31. A free venue hire for afternoon functions that are done by 6pm. A matched wine deal for the first 10 function bookings.

The incentive should be margin-positive or margin-neutral — not a discount that erodes your December margin, but an add-on that rewards the booking behaviour you want (early, committed, deposit-paying).

## Christmas Walk-In Versus Function Balance

The tension in December is between maximising function revenue (high-value, certain) and maintaining enough walk-in and a la carte capacity for your regular customers who can't get a seat because the restaurant is entirely booked with functions.

The right balance depends on your venue's identity and your regular base. A neighbourhood restaurant that fills with locals year-round should be careful not to lose December to corporate functions entirely — those locals will remember in January whether you still had space for them. A venue that's primarily event-focused can optimise December almost entirely for functions.
