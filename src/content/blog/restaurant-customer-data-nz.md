A typical Auckland restaurant serves 800–1,500 people per month. In a year, that is 10,000–18,000 individual diner interactions. How many of those people could you contact if you needed to fill a Tuesday night in two weeks? For most NZ restaurants, the answer is close to zero.

This is the customer data problem in NZ hospitality, and it is worth taking seriously.

## What Customer Data Actually Means for Restaurants

Customer data for restaurants is straightforward: a name, an email address, and a booking history. Nothing complex. Not a CRM with behavioural segmentation and predictive analytics.

Just a list of people who have eaten at your venue and given you permission to contact them.

That list is one of the most commercially valuable assets a restaurant can own. A venue with a list of 500 engaged local customers can:

- Fill a quiet Tuesday with one email
- Announce a new menu and convert a percentage into bookings that week
- Run a pre-launch for a special event and sell out before external promotion
- Weather a slow winter period by activating their most loyal segment directly

A venue with zero customer contact data can do none of these things. It is entirely dependent on foot traffic, platforms, and the algorithm of whatever social network it uses.

## How the Platform Model Removes Customer Data From Venues

Every booking through First Table is a booking where the customer's data belongs to First Table. Every booking through Dimmi is a booking where the customer's data sits in Dimmi's database. These platforms are not being malicious — the customer data is their business model. Their value to investors is partly the database of diner preferences and behaviours they are accumulating.

For venues, the implication is straightforward: every booking through these platforms is a cover that generates revenue but no lasting customer asset. Do 400 covers in a month through a commission-based platform? You have the revenue. You do not have the 400 contacts.

Over time, venues that rely primarily on platforms for bookings are building revenue without building relationships. The revenue is temporary — it requires the platform to keep sending customers. The relationship would be permanent — once a customer is in your contact list, they can be contacted for years.

## The Simple Math of Direct Customer Contact

Assume 30% of your monthly covers are through platforms that do not share customer data. That is 240 covers per month in a 800-cover venue.

Over 12 months: 2,880 customer interactions you could have had contact for. At a conservative 15% conversion to engaged email subscribers: 432 contacts you could have been building.

At a 35% email open rate and 10% booking conversion from emails: 15 incremental bookings per email, per month, at zero marginal platform cost.

That is the commercial value of the data you are not collecting.

## How to Start Building Customer Data

**Use a booking system that gives you the data**: The single most impactful change. [LocalFeed](https://localfeed.app) provides venue owners with customer contact information from every booking. No platform holds it. It comes to you. This is structurally different from platforms where the booking data stays in the platform's database.

**Capture walk-ins and bar customers**: A table sign with a QR code to join your email list. A verbal ask for contact details when taking a name for a wait list. A competition or giveaway requiring an email address. The specific mechanism matters less than having one.

**Capture from existing loyalty touchpoints**: If you have a stamp card or any kind of loyalty interaction, that is an opportunity to capture contact details.

**Website signup**: A genuine reason to sign up — early access to events, monthly specials, seasonal menu updates. A well-designed signup form converts a percentage of website visitors into contacts.

## Managing the List You Build

A contact list is only valuable if you use it. The venues that build large lists and do nothing with them have wasted their infrastructure.

The minimum viable usage:

- A monthly email to the list (not a discount — a genuine update about what is happening at the venue)
- An event announcement when you have something worth promoting
- An occasional quiet-session fill message ("Tuesday is looking quiet this week — here is what is good right now")

Monthly contact, consistently, maintained for twelve months, turns a contact list into a meaningful revenue lever.

## The Competitive Advantage

In a competitive NZ hospitality market, the venue that can fill its own tables through direct communication has a structural advantage over venues that cannot. Platform algorithms change. Discovery patterns shift. Google ranking fluctuates.

A direct relationship with 500 local customers who trust your venue and open your emails does not fluctuate. It is yours.
