The NZ hospitality industry loses experienced staff at rates that most other industries would find alarming. Skilled chefs and senior front-of-house staff are genuinely scarce. When they leave, the cost — direct and indirect — is significant, and the disruption to your product and culture can be lasting.

Retention is not just a human resources question. It is a business economics question.

## The Real Cost of Staff Turnover

When an experienced team member leaves, the actual replacement cost includes:

- **Advertising**: Job board fees, recruiters if used ($2,000–$5,000)
- **Lost productivity**: The period between their departure and their replacement being fully functional. During this time, remaining staff absorb extra load and your product consistency suffers.
- **Training time**: A new hire at any level requires weeks to months to reach the productivity of the person they replaced.
- **Service quality impact**: Customers notice when the team changes. Regulars who were loyal partly to the people they knew may become less frequent.

Conservative estimates for replacing an experienced kitchen or floor staff member in NZ: $3,000–$8,000 when everything is accounted for. A venue that loses four or five good people per year is carrying a significant hidden cost.

## Why NZ Hospitality Staff Leave

The industry has chronic retention problems for structural reasons that are worth understanding:

**Low wages relative to workload**: NZ's minimum wage has risen significantly, but the ratio of hourly rate to physical and mental demands of hospitality work remains uncomfortable. Staff compare their take-home to equivalent-effort work in other industries and often find hospitality wanting.

**Unsocial hours without adequate compensation**: Weekends, late nights, and split shifts are inherent to hospitality. Many venues treat this as accepted without providing additional compensation or acknowledgment.

**Limited career progression**: Many NZ hospitality businesses are small enough that career progression is structurally limited. A great sous chef cannot be promoted to head chef in a venue that already has one and is not opening new locations.

**Toxic culture and poor management**: This is the most frequently cited reason for departure in exit interviews. Not the wages. The management.

**Physical burnout**: Hospitality is physically demanding. The industry has a burnout problem, and the venues that ignore it lose good people to other industries entirely.

## What the Best-Retaining Venues Do Differently

**Pay above the minimum**: Not dramatically, but meaningfully. A $2–$3 per hour premium above minimum wage for experienced staff creates financial loyalty that is surprisingly durable. In the context of your labour cost, it is a modest increase. In the context of your staff member's decision to look for another job, it is often decisive.

**Predictable scheduling**: Irregular, late-confirmed rosters create stress that accumulates into dissatisfaction. Venues that confirm rosters 10–14 days in advance, maintain consistent days off, and avoid last-minute changes are rated significantly better by their teams.

**Genuine acknowledgment**: The hospitality businesses with the best retention rates pay attention to their people as people. They notice when someone is having a hard week. They acknowledge good work publicly and specifically. They remember what matters to individual team members.

**A pathway for good people**: Even if your venue is not expanding, you can offer progression through additional responsibility, senior shift management roles, mentorship of junior staff, and engagement in menu development. People stay when they feel they are growing.

**A culture that makes the difficult parts bearable**: Hospitality will always involve difficult customers, difficult services, and difficult days. The difference between venues with high and low retention is often whether the culture around those difficult moments is one of collective resilience or individual blame.

## The Roster: The Most Operational Thing You Can Fix

More than wages and more than culture, inconsistent and unpredictable scheduling is the single most correctable retention problem in NZ hospitality.

A staff member who cannot plan their personal life because their roster changes week to week will eventually choose an employer who gives them predictability. This is not complicated — it requires the operational discipline to forecast your trading accurately enough to roster 10–14 days out and stick to it.

The venues that have solved their rostering discipline retain staff longer than equivalent venues that have not.

## The Exit Interview

When a good team member gives notice, ask them clearly and genuinely why they are leaving. Not as a formality. As data. The answer is almost always more actionable than venue owners expect, and it often reveals something about the culture or operations that can be changed before the next person leaves for the same reason.
