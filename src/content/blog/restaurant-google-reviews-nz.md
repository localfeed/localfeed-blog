A one-star improvement in a restaurant's Google rating correlates with a 5–9% increase in revenue, according to research across multiple hospitality markets. In NZ, where Google is the primary search tool for restaurant discovery, your Google rating and review count are among the most commercially important metrics in your business.

Most NZ restaurants manage them passively. That is a significant missed opportunity.

## Why Google Reviews Matter More Than Other Platforms

TripAdvisor, Zomato, and Facebook reviews all matter. Google reviews matter most because:

1. **Search integration**: Google reviews appear directly in search results, in the Google local pack, and on Google Maps. No other platform gets this integration with search.

2. **Volume and trust**: Google reviews have become the default recommendation platform for NZ consumers. When someone wants to know if a venue is good, they look at its Google rating before anything else.

3. **Algorithm signal**: Google uses review count, recency, rating, and engagement (review responses) as ranking signals for local search. More reviews, regularly, with responses, means higher ranking.

## The Right Way to Ask for Reviews

**In-person ask**: Train your floor team to ask satisfied tables at the end of service. The script should be simple and specific: "If you enjoyed tonight, we would love a Google review — it makes a real difference to a small venue like ours." The key words are "Google review" (not "online review") and the framing of why it matters. People who feel they helped a local business leave more reviews than people who feel they completed a task for a corporation.

**QR code on receipt**: A QR code that goes directly to your Google review page removes friction. On the receipt, or on a small card at the table, this converts a meaningful percentage of satisfied diners into reviewers.

**Follow-up email**: If you have the booking email, a next-day thank-you email with a review link converts some percentage. Keep it short: "Thank you for coming in last night. If you enjoyed it, a Google review would mean a lot to us: [link]."

**What not to do**: Do not offer discounts or free items in exchange for reviews. Google's policy prohibits incentivised reviews and the practice is visible to other consumers reading reviews, which undermines trust.

## How to Respond to Reviews (Positive and Negative)

Response rate is a ranking signal. Venues that respond to 90%+ of reviews rank better than venues that respond to 20%. Beyond the algorithm, how you respond shapes the impression of every future customer who reads the review.

**Responding to positive reviews:**

- Acknowledge specifically what the reviewer mentioned
- Use the customer's name if available
- Keep it short — three to five sentences
- Do not copy-paste the same response to every review (Google recognises templated responses)

Example: "Thank you so much, Emma — really glad the duck worked for you. It is one we have had on the menu for a while and it is always great to know when it lands. Hope to see you again soon."

**Responding to negative reviews:**

This is where most venues fail. The wrong response (defensive, dismissive, arguing with the reviewer) is visible to every potential customer who reads it and creates a worse impression than the original complaint.

The right approach:

1. Thank them for the feedback
2. Acknowledge the specific issue without being defensive
3. Apologise genuinely if appropriate
4. Invite them to contact you directly to resolve it
5. Keep emotion out of it

Example: "Thank you for taking the time to leave feedback. I'm sorry the wait time wasn't what we would have hoped — it was a busy Saturday and that's no excuse. We'd love the chance to do better. Please feel free to reach out to us directly at [email] if you'd like to discuss it."

The audience for this response is not the reviewer. It is the next 1,000 people who read it.

## Getting from 20 Reviews to 200

The gap between 20 Google reviews and 200 Google reviews, at similar ratings, significantly impacts search ranking and consumer trust. Getting from 20 to 200 is a six-to-twelve month project with a consistent approach.

What that looks like in practice:
- Ask every satisfied table, in-person, every service
- QR code on every receipt for three months as a specific campaign
- Follow-up email via your booking system for every booking with an email address
- Respond to every new review within 24 hours
- Track review count weekly as a team metric

A venue doing 200 covers per week that asks every table should be adding 5–15 new reviews per week if a fraction of asks convert. At that rate, 200 reviews is achievable in under six months.

## Handling Fake or Unfair Reviews

Genuinely fake reviews can be reported through Google Business Profile for removal. The bar for removal is high — the review must violate Google's content policies, which fake reviews from competitors or malicious actors do.

For unfair (but technically permitted) reviews: respond professionally, state the factual situation briefly, and let it go. Other reviewers and potential customers read these exchanges. A measured, professional response to an unfair review demonstrates more credibility than no response.
