A restaurant can be fully booked and still run out of cash. It is one of the most misunderstood features of the hospitality business model, and it is why venues that appear to be thriving can close suddenly without obvious warning.

Understanding cash flow is not an accounting exercise. It is an operational survival skill.

## Why Hospitality Cash Flow Is Different

Most product businesses have a simple cash cycle: buy inventory, sell product, collect payment, repeat. Hospitality is more complicated.

You pay for your ingredients before the customer arrives. You pay rent whether the tables are full or not. Your staff costs are fixed weekly regardless of how many covers you serve. The cash goes out in advance, often significantly in advance, of the revenue that is supposed to cover it.

Then layer on top: the Saturday night that was supposed to be your best week and had four no-shows. The supplier invoice that arrived on the 28th when the long weekend meant your trading was weak. The new equipment purchase that you knew was coming but the timing was brutal.

NZ hospitality businesses operate on thin margins — typically 3–9% net profit on revenue. In that environment, a week of poor trading does not just hurt. It can create a cash deficit that takes a month to claw back.

## The Metrics That Matter

**Weekly cash position**: What is in the account right now, what goes out this week, what comes in this week, and what is the position at end of week? Running this weekly is not optional. It is the single most important thing an owner-operator can do.

**Cost of sales percentage**: Food and labour together. In NZ, industry benchmarks are 28–32% for food cost and 28–35% for labour. Combined above 65% leaves dangerously thin contribution margins. Know your number every week.

**Fixed cost coverage**: At what revenue level do you cover your fixed costs (rent, utilities, base labour, insurance)? This is your break-even. Trading below it in any week means you are drawing down cash reserves. How many weeks can you do that before it becomes critical?

**Debtors and creditors**: If you have trade accounts, when do invoices fall due? A mismatch between when you pay suppliers and when revenue comes in can create a cash squeeze even in a profitable week.

## The Seasonal Trap

NZ hospitality has strong seasonality. December–January is strong. July–August is soft. The trap many venues fall into is spending their December surplus as if it represents normal trading, then hitting a cash wall in the quieter months.

The fix is a rolling 13-week cash flow projection, updated weekly. It forces you to see the July problem in March while you still have options. By the time July arrives and the cash is gone, the options are gone with it.

## Platform Timing and Cash Flow

Booking platforms that charge commission create a cash timing issue worth understanding. If a platform takes 10–15% commission and pays out monthly, you have been fronting that cash through the month. In a high-volume month, the lag between earning revenue and receiving net payout can be several thousand dollars.

Flat-fee platforms change this calculus. [LocalFeed](https://localfeed.app) charges $10 per week flat — no commission, no percentage, no monthly payout lag. If you do $20,000 in platform-assisted revenue in a month, you receive $20,000, not $20,000 minus 12% minus the processing fee.

The cash flow impact of platform structure is rarely discussed but genuinely significant at volume.

## Practical Cash Flow Management

**Negotiate supplier terms**: Pushing from 7-day to 14-day or 30-day terms with key suppliers smooths cash flow significantly. Most suppliers will negotiate for a reliable paying account.

**Manage the payroll cycle**: Weekly payroll creates a regular cash drain. Understanding exactly when it falls and ensuring trading revenue covers it before the draw is operational discipline.

**Build a cash buffer**: The advice is simple and the execution is hard: maintain a minimum cash reserve equal to 4–6 weeks of fixed costs. In practice, most NZ hospitality businesses are running much thinner than this. Even two weeks of fixed costs in reserve changes the stress level of operating a venue.

**Review cost of sales weekly**: Food cost that creeps from 30% to 34% over three months is the slow bleed that does not appear as a crisis until it is. Weekly cost of sales review catches drift before it becomes structural.

## The Honest Conversation

Most NZ venue owners are brilliant at hospitality and uncomfortable with financial management. The cash flow spreadsheet does not feel like running a restaurant. But it is what keeps the restaurant running.

If numbers are not your strength, consider a hospitality-specific accountant or bookkeeper who works across multiple venues and can contextualise your numbers against industry benchmarks. The cost of that service is almost always returned in the decisions it enables.
