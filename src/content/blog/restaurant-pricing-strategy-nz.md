Menu pricing is the highest-leverage decision a venue makes. A 10% increase in your average menu price, all else equal, delivers roughly $25,000–$60,000 in additional annual gross profit for a mid-sized NZ restaurant. A 10% improvement in your food cost delivers a similar result. The difference is that pricing takes ten minutes to change and food cost reduction takes months.

Most NZ restaurant operators underprice their menus. Here is how to do it correctly.

## The Foundation: Food Cost Percentage

Every item on your menu should be costed. The actual cost of ingredients per serve, accurately calculated including portion size, waste, and any components that are shared across dishes.

The target food cost percentage varies by venue type and dish category:

| Category | Target food cost % |
|---|---|
| Bread and pastry | 15–22% |
| Salads and starters | 20–28% |
| Proteins (chicken, pork) | 28–35% |
| Proteins (premium: lamb, beef, seafood) | 32–40% |
| Pasta and grains | 18–28% |
| Desserts | 22–30% |
| Cocktails | 18–24% |
| Wine by the glass | 22–28% |

These are guides, not rules. A high-margin pasta dish subsidises a lower-margin protein dish on a menu that averages 30% food cost. What matters is the blended food cost across the full menu, not each item in isolation.

## The Pricing Formula

**Menu price = Ingredient cost ÷ Target food cost percentage**

If your burger has $8 in ingredients and you target 30% food cost: $8 ÷ 0.30 = $26.67, rounded to $27.

This formula is the floor. The actual price can be above this if the market supports it. Setting a price below the formula price because "it seems too high" is how venues quietly erode their margins over months.

## The Underpricing Psychology in NZ

New Zealand venue owners have a well-documented tendency to underprice relative to market and relative to cost. The reasons:

**Fear of losing customers**: "If I charge $32 for the salmon, people won't order it." In practice, a $3–5 price increase on a well-executed dish rarely changes ordering behaviour significantly. The customers who will not pay $32 also would not have paid $29.

**Anchoring to old prices**: Prices set in 2022 or 2023 based on ingredient costs from that time are now structurally wrong given ingredient inflation. If you have not reviewed pricing in 18 months, you have already absorbed the ingredient cost increase as a margin reduction.

**Visible competitor pricing**: "The restaurant down the road charges $28, so I should too." If the restaurant down the road is also underpriced, you are following them into the same margin problem.

## How to Raise Prices Without Losing Customers

**Raise gradually, not suddenly.** A $4 increase at menu reprint is less noticeable than a $4 increase announced on a sign. Menus change seasonally. Build the pricing review into the seasonal menu change.

**Change the dish, not just the price.** Adding a component, updating the description, or repositioning a dish on the menu gives visual justification for a higher price even when the change is modest.

**Lead with value, not discount.** Venues that position on value rather than cheapness can raise prices without losing the value perception. If your $32 salmon is clearly excellent and the service is warm, customers are not comparing it to the $29 salmon down the road.

**Test on new items.** When adding new dishes, price them at the level you wish your existing menu was at. This gradually normalises higher price points across your menu without requiring an immediate full menu reprint.

## Dynamic Pricing: Weekday vs Weekend

A legitimate strategy that is underused in NZ hospitality: price differently for different sessions.

A two-course prix-fixe at $45 on Tuesday is not a discounted version of your Friday menu. It is a different product designed for a different session. This allows you to offer genuine value for quiet sessions without undermining weekend pricing.

This is distinct from heavy discounting through platforms. A platform-mandated 50% off food is a margin crisis. A designed weekday product at a considered price is a strategy.

## The Annual Pricing Review

Set a date in your calendar: once a year, cost every menu item from scratch using current ingredient prices. Compare the output to your current menu prices. Any item where current ingredient cost has pushed food cost above 38% needs either repricing or reformulation.

This exercise takes half a day and can return $10,000–$30,000 in recovered margin for venues that have been operating on drift.
