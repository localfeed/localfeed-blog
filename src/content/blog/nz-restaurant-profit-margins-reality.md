The person who tells you the restaurant industry has great margins has not seen the P&L. The person who tells you it is impossible to make money in NZ hospo is not running a tight operation. The reality sits in a specific range, depends significantly on venue type and cost structure, and is determined by decisions that most venue operators can control more than they think.

## What NZ venue profit margins actually look like

Net profit margin — what you keep after all costs — varies significantly by venue type:

**Full-service dinner restaurant**: 3%–9% net profit margin on revenue. A Wellington restaurant doing $1.2 million annually is keeping $36,000–$108,000. The range is wide because the cost drivers — rent, labour, food cost — vary significantly by location and management quality.

**Cafe**: 4%–11% net profit margin. Cafes with strong beverage sales (coffee at 70%–80% gross margin) and controlled food cost tend to run above restaurants. A $600,000 cafe is keeping $24,000–$66,000 annually.

**Bar and pub**: 8%–16% net profit margin. Beverage margin is stronger than food margin, and many bars have lower kitchen labour costs than restaurants. A Queenstown pub doing $2 million annually might keep $160,000–$320,000.

**Food truck and takeaway**: 6%–15% net profit margin. Lower rent and no dine-in labour. Constrained by throughput capacity.

These are net margin figures — after rent, labour, food cost, utilities, insurance, repairs, merchant fees, platform costs, and everything else.

**STAT:** 6.2% · Average net profit margin across NZ restaurant category venues in 2025, based on IRD and Statistics NZ hospo sector data. For a venue doing $800,000, that is $49,600. A single wage increase, a rent review, or a platform fee arrangement can move this number materially.

## The cost stack that produces the margin

Starting from revenue and working down to net profit, the typical NZ restaurant P&L looks like:

- **Revenue**: 100%
- **Food and beverage cost** (CoGS): 28%–35%
- **Gross profit**: 65%–72%
- **Labour** (kitchen + floor + management): 28%–38%
- **Rent and occupancy**: 8%–15%
- **Utilities**: 2%–4%
- **Marketing and platform fees**: 1%–4%
- **Insurance, repairs, admin, merchant fees**: 2%–4%
- **Net profit**: 3%–12% (the remainder)

The three lines that account for most of the variance between venues running at 3% and those running at 11% are labour, rent, and food cost. These are also the three lines with the most management levers.

> A restaurant where one of the big three — food cost, labour, or rent — is running 3 percentage points above target is running at the bottom of the profit range. A restaurant where all three are at or below target is running at the top.

## The levers that actually move margin

**Food cost**: the most controllable line. A kitchen tracking weekly and managing waste actively runs 2–4 percentage points lower than one that does not. On a $400,000 food revenue venue, 3 percentage points is $12,000 annually. The [food cost control guide for NZ restaurants](/blog/food-cost-control-nz-restaurant) covers the mechanics.

**Labour cost percentage**: the ratio of labour cost to revenue. This improves through scheduling discipline (no overstaffing slow services), cross-training (fewer specialist roles required), and service model decisions (is full table service the right model for your quieter sessions?).

**Platform fees and forced discounts**: this is the cost line most NZ venue operators are not calculating fully. A 3% commission on $200,000 of platform-sourced revenue is $6,000 annually. A 50% forced discount on 40 early covers per month at $80 food spend is $19,200 annually in margin reduction. These are material costs that directly reduce net profit.

Moving covers from commission-based platforms to commission-free channels like [LocalFeed](https://localfeed.co.nz) at $10/week flat reduces this cost line without requiring any change to the customer-facing product.

**NOTE:** Run your P&L as a percentage of revenue, not just as dollar amounts. The percentage view reveals instantly whether a cost line is in or out of range. Labour at $120,000 on a $500,000 revenue year is 24% — good. Labour at $120,000 on a $350,000 revenue year is 34% — a problem. The dollar amount is the same.

## The rent negotiation most NZ venue operators do not attempt

Rent is often treated as a fixed cost that cannot be negotiated. In practice, commercial leases in NZ have more flexibility than most tenants realise, particularly at renewal and in the current environment where commercial landlords are aware of hospo conditions.

Actions worth attempting at renewal:

- A rent review linked to revenue performance (a percentage of revenue rather than a fixed amount protects you in slow periods)
- A rent-free fitout period at renewal in exchange for a longer lease term
- Inclusion of outgoings that are sometimes charged separately (water, rates, building insurance)
- A right of first refusal on adjacent space

A $1,000/month reduction in rent is $12,000 per year — roughly 1.5–2 percentage points of net margin on a $700,000 revenue venue. It is worth having the conversation.

**FACT:** Zero commission on food revenue. $10/week after 20 bookings. 75% of no-show fees go to the venue.

NZ restaurant profit margins are achievable. They require discipline on all three of the major cost drivers simultaneously — food cost, labour, and occupancy — combined with a thoughtful approach to platform fees and revenue channels. The venues running at 8–12% net margin in NZ hospo are not operating in easier conditions than the ones running at 3–4%. They are managing the same costs more precisely.
