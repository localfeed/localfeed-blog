A table of twelve on a Thursday night is worth more to your business than two tables of six on a Friday. The margin is the same but the Thursday table fills a session that might otherwise be half-empty, and the groups booking brought it regardless of the day.

Groups bookings are the single best quiet-session strategy for most NZ restaurants. They are also the most under-capitalised opportunity in NZ hospitality.

## Why Groups Bookings Are Commercially Valuable

The economics of a groups booking versus regular table service:

**Day and time flexibility**: Groups are booked around the group's occasion — a birthday, a team dinner, a corporate event, a farewell. The occasion is the fixed point, not the day. This makes groups bookings available on Tuesdays and Thursdays in ways that individual diner bookings are not.

**Minimum spend protection**: A well-structured groups booking includes a minimum spend or set menu requirement. A table of 10 with a $100 per head minimum generates guaranteed revenue before the first guest arrives.

**Lower no-show risk**: Groups have social accountability that individual bookings lack. The person who organised the dinner for twelve people will not simply not show up.

**Kitchen efficiency**: A set menu for a group is operationally simpler than full à la carte service for the same number of covers. Less ordering complexity, more predictable prep, fewer kitchen errors.

## The Groups That Exist and Are Looking for Venues

Most of the bookings that fill quiet midweek sessions come from groups with a purpose. They are not looking for the cheapest option. They are looking for a venue that is easy to book, can handle their numbers, and will not let them down.

**Corporate teams and offices**: Team lunches, client dinners, end-of-quarter celebrations, management offsites. These happen regularly throughout the year, including on weekdays. The booker is often a PA or office manager looking for an easy, reliable option they can trust.

**Birthday dinners**: Groups of 8–16 celebrating a milestone birthday. Usually on a weekend but frequently happy to do a Thursday if the venue is right.

**Sports teams and clubs**: Post-match dinners, end-of-season celebrations. Often large, less formal, high food and beverage spend.

**Book clubs, professional groups, associations**: Recurring small group meetings that happen weekly or monthly. Reliable repeat bookings for the venue that hosts them.

**Corporate away days and events**: Full venue hire or exclusive sections for a half-day or full day with food and drink included.

## Building a Groups Product

Before marketing to groups, define the product:

**Groups capacity**: How many can you accommodate in a private or semi-private space? What is the minimum size you will take as a groups booking versus regular table service?

**Set menu options**: Offer two or three set menus at different price points. A two-course at $55 per head, a three-course at $75 per head, a premium three-course with paired wines at $110 per head. Clear options make the booking conversation easy.

**Minimum spend**: State it clearly. A table of 8 with a $600 minimum, or $75 per head — whichever is higher. Vague policies create difficult conversations later.

**Booking process**: How does a group enquire? A dedicated email address, a short booking form on your website, a phone number that gets answered. Make the process visible and frictionless.

## Marketing to Groups

**Your website**: A dedicated groups or private dining page with capacity, set menu options, minimum spend, and a clear contact process. This page should be easy to find — in your navigation, not buried in the footer.

**Google Business Profile**: Add "private dining" and "group bookings" to your service list. Some searches for "restaurant private dining Auckland" are coming from high-intent group bookers.

**Direct corporate outreach**: This is where most venues fail to act. A personal email to executive assistants and office managers at businesses within 1–2km of your venue, introducing your groups offering, generates direct enquiries. It is unglamorous and effective.

**LinkedIn**: For venues with a corporate groups focus, LinkedIn posts about your groups and events offering reach the exact audience that books corporate dinners.

**Follow-up with past groups**: A group that had a good experience at your venue last November is the warmest possible prospect for a booking this November. Keeping records and following up proactively converts repeat groups bookings at very low cost.

## The Enquiry Response

The gap between receiving a groups enquiry and responding is where most bookings are lost. A booker researching venues on Monday will contact three or four options. The first to respond with a clear proposal is almost always the one who gets the booking.

Target same-day response. Provide a clear proposal: date, set menu options, pricing, deposit requirement, and a simple way to confirm. The booker should not need to chase you.
