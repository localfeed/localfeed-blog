The word "upselling" has a bad reputation in hospitality because it is usually done badly. A staff member who reads a script ("would you like to add a dessert today?"), delivers it to every table at the same point in the service regardless of context, and moves on regardless of the answer is not upselling. They are going through a motion. The kind of upselling that improves average spend without making customers feel managed is something different — and it is worth training specifically.

## The difference between upselling that works and upselling that damages the experience

An upsell that works happens when a team member makes a specific, genuine recommendation based on what the customer has already ordered or expressed interest in. "The lamb shoulder tonight is excellent — if you enjoy lamb, it is worth considering" from a server who has eaten the dish is an upsell. It is also hospitality.

An upsell that damages the experience is a scripted add-on delivered at a predetermined point in the meal regardless of whether the customer is interested or engaged. "Can I get you started with some sparkling water tonight?" before the customer has sat down fully is not upselling. It is a checkout prompt wearing a hospitality uniform.

NZ diners are good at telling the difference. A server who genuinely knows the menu and makes a specific recommendation earns trust. A server running through a checklist loses it.

**STAT:** 22% · Average increase in table spend when upselling is delivered through genuine recommendation rather than scripted prompting, based on NZ hospo training outcomes. The mechanism is trust — genuine recommendations convert; scripted ones create resistance.

## The high-margin upsells worth training

Not all upsells are equal. The ones worth training your team on are the ones with strong margin impact and high customer acceptance rates.

**Beverage pairings**: "This dish works well with the Marlborough Sauvignon — would you like a glass?" has a high conversion rate when the server knows the pairing is genuinely good, and beverage margin is typically stronger than food margin. A glass of wine at $14 with a 65% margin contributes $9.10 gross profit from one suggestion.

**Sharing plates or additions**: "The truffle fries are worth sharing between two" at the right moment — when a table has ordered mains and has space in the budget — converts frequently and adds a high-margin item.

**Upgrading the coffee**: "Would you prefer a long black or a flat white?" is not an upsell. "Would you like to try our single-origin espresso today — it's a Rwandan natural we just got in" is an upsell that also serves as a coffee education moment. Coffee is your highest-margin category. Training staff to describe the coffee offering specifically increases uptake.

**The dessert at the right moment**: asking about dessert while clearing the main course, not when the customer is mid-bite with their partner, converts at 25–35% when the team member can describe one or two options specifically. "We have a chocolate ganache tonight that the kitchen has been making for about three years — it is on the menu for good reason" is a recommendation. "Can I get you a dessert menu?" is not.

> The upsell that converts is indistinguishable from hospitality. The customer does not feel sold to. They feel looked after.

**NOTE:** The one upsell that almost never works and consistently annoys NZ customers: asking if they want a side of chips or salad before they have established whether they want those things. If the menu includes them, they will order them. If they do not order them, they do not want them. Adding an unsolicited prompt to an order damages the flow.

## Training the team to upsell without a script

Scripts do not work because they remove the element that makes a recommendation trustworthy: specificity and genuine knowledge.

The training approach that produces genuine upsellers:

**The team eats the menu**: a staff member who has tasted the lamb shoulder and the chocolate ganache can describe both in specific, honest terms. A staff member who has not can only read from a card. Schedule staff meals and make eating the menu a part of onboarding.

**The recommendation moment is taught, not scripted**: training should teach staff to read the table — is the couple on a date and relaxed, or in a hurry and distracted? — and to time the recommendation accordingly. A relaxed couple is a candidate for a wine pairing and a dessert suggestion. A table who have asked for their bill before they finish is not.

**Knowledge of margin is optional for the team**: staff do not need to know the margin on the wine pairing to make a good recommendation. They need to know the wine is good. The margin management is a management task.

## The average spend lift that compounds

A NZ restaurant that increases average spend by $8 per cover through genuine recommendation-based upselling does the following to its annual revenue:

- 60 covers per service × $8 × 5 services per week = $2,400 per week
- Annualised: $124,800 in additional revenue
- At 70% beverage gross margin on the upsold items: $87,360 in additional gross profit

These numbers assume a genuine, consistent programme — not a one-week training push that fades when the head chef goes on leave.

**FACT:** Zero commission on food revenue. $10/week after 20 bookings. 75% of no-show fees go to the venue.

Upselling in NZ hospitality is not a sales technique. It is an expression of genuine knowledge about the menu and genuine interest in the customer's experience. When it is trained as the former, it fails. When it is embedded as part of the latter, the average spend lift is consistent, the customer experience improves, and the team develops a skill that serves them throughout their hospo career.
