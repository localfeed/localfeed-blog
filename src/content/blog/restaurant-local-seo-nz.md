When someone in Auckland types "dinner tonight Ponsonby" or "best café Wellington CBD" into Google, the venues appearing in the top three results get the majority of clicks. Most NZ restaurants are not those venues, and they do not need to be — but understanding what puts a venue in that position is the highest-leverage free marketing activity available to a venue owner.

## How Google Decides Which Restaurants to Show

Google's local search ranking for restaurants depends on three main factors:

**Relevance**: Does your Google Business Profile clearly describe what you are, where you are, and what experience you offer? A profile that says "restaurant" ranks worse than one that says "casual Italian restaurant in Ponsonby, Auckland, with wood-fired pizza and natural wine."

**Distance**: Google factors in how close the venue is to the searcher. You cannot change your physical location, but ensuring your location data is precise and consistent across all platforms matters.

**Prominence**: How well-known and trusted is the venue based on reviews, website authority, mentions across the web, and engagement on the Google Business Profile?

Of these three, prominence is the most actionable and the most neglected.

## Google Business Profile: The Foundation

Your Google Business Profile is the single most important local SEO asset you have. It appears in search results, Google Maps, and the local pack at the top of search pages. It is free. Most NZ restaurant profiles are under-optimised.

**Essential optimisations:**

**Complete every field**: Business name, category (be specific: "Italian restaurant" not just "restaurant"), address, phone, website, hours. Incomplete profiles rank lower than complete ones.

**Add photos regularly**: Google's algorithm rewards active profiles. Uploading at least one new photo per week signals that the venue is current and active. Interior, exterior, food, staff, specials — all are valid.

**Collect and respond to reviews**: Reviews are a direct ranking signal. A venue with 200 reviews at 4.3 stars almost always outranks a venue with 20 reviews at 4.8 stars. Quantity matters. Responding to reviews also signals to Google that the profile is actively managed.

**Write a complete description**: Use your business description to include your suburb name, your cuisine type, your key offer, and any distinctive features. Natural keyword inclusion (not stuffing) helps Google match your profile to relevant searches.

**Use Google Posts**: Post weekly about specials, events, menu changes. Posts appear on your profile and in some search results. They also signal an active, current venue.

## Website SEO for Restaurants

Your website matters less than your Google Business Profile for local discovery, but it still matters.

**Key pages to have and optimise:**

**Homepage**: Should clearly state who you are, where you are (suburb and city), and what you offer. Include your suburb name in the page title and first paragraph.

**Menu page**: Google can read your menu and may surface specific dishes in search results. Keep your online menu current.

**Location / contact page**: Include your full address (not just a map embed), your phone number as text, and your opening hours. These should match exactly what is on your Google Business Profile. Inconsistency between your website and your Google profile reduces ranking.

**Reviews and press**: If you have been featured in local media or food publications, reference this on your website. External mentions are a prominence signal.

## Building Local Citations

A citation is any mention of your business name, address, and phone number on another website. Consistent citations across multiple platforms signal to Google that your business is legitimate and well-established.

The platforms to be listed on:

- Google Business Profile (the most important)
- Apple Maps
- Yelp NZ
- TripAdvisor
- Facebook Business Page
- Zomato NZ
- AA Tourism (if applicable)
- Local business directories relevant to your suburb or city

The key is consistency. If your address appears differently on three different platforms (road vs. street abbreviation, etc.), this creates a small negative signal. Audit your citations annually.

## Getting Reviews Strategically

Reviews are the most impactful single action for local restaurant SEO, and most NZ venues have a passive approach to collecting them.

Active approaches that work:

**Verbal ask at end of service**: Train your floor staff to mention Google reviews to satisfied tables. "If you enjoyed tonight, we would really appreciate a Google review — it makes a big difference for a small venue." This works better than printed cards.

**QR code on the receipt or at the table**: A direct link to your Google review page, accessible via QR code. Reduces friction significantly.

**Email follow-up**: If you have a customer's email from their booking, a follow-up the next day thanking them and including a link to leave a review converts some percentage into reviews.

Do not offer incentives for reviews — it violates Google's policy. Genuine requests for genuine reviews are both ethical and effective.

## The Result

A Google Business Profile that is actively managed, with 100+ reviews, weekly photo posts, and complete information, combined with a well-structured website and consistent citations, puts most NZ restaurants in a strong position for local search.

The investment is about 30 minutes per week. The return, in new customer traffic that does not require paid advertising, is significant.
