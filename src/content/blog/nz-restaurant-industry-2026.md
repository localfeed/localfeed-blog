New Zealand's restaurant and café industry is in a period of structural adjustment. The combination of post-pandemic behaviour changes, persistent cost inflation, and a shift in how diners discover and book venues has created conditions where the strategies that worked in 2019 are no longer sufficient.

This is not a crisis. It is a transition. The venues navigating it well are growing. Those waiting for conditions to normalise back to a pre-2020 baseline are struggling.

Here is what the data shows and what it means for NZ operators.

## The Closure Rate

NZ hospitality closures have been running above historical averages since 2022. The causes are broadly understood:

- Fixed costs (rent, base labour, utilities) rose faster than operators could offset through pricing
- Post-pandemic consumer behaviour changed: more discretionary caution, more last-minute booking patterns, higher tolerance for cancelling
- Supply chain disruptions pushed ingredient costs up 10–20% across most categories
- Wage growth, while necessary and appropriate, compressed margins in an industry already operating thin

The closures have disproportionately hit the middle market — mid-price, mid-quality venues that cannot differentiate on either exceptional value or exceptional experience. The premium market and the casual/affordable market have both shown more resilience.

## Where the Costs Have Gone

The structure of NZ restaurant operating costs in 2026, compared to 2022:

**Labour**: Minimum wage increases since 2020 have added 15–20% to the base labour cost for hospitality businesses at the lower pay band. Senior kitchen and service staff have seen higher wage growth still, driven by genuine scarcity.

**Food costs**: Produce inflation in New Zealand has been uneven but broadly upward. Protein costs (beef, lamb, seafood) have been particularly volatile. Overall food cost in well-managed NZ venues has risen 8–15% in ingredient price, often not fully passed through to menu pricing.

**Rent**: Commercial rent in Auckland and Wellington CBD locations has continued to increase in most categories. The commercial leasing market has provided less relief to hospitality than many operators expected post-pandemic.

**Platform fees**: Not a new cost but a growing one. The percentage of bookings going through commission-based platforms has increased, creating an aggregate cost that is now meaningful in many venues' P&L.

## How Diner Behaviour Has Changed

Several genuine shifts in NZ diner behaviour have become structural rather than pandemic-temporary:

**Later booking windows**: The average time between booking and visit has shortened significantly. Bookings made weeks in advance still exist but are less common. More diners are booking 24–72 hours out or same-day. This creates inventory management challenges for venues.

**Higher no-show rates**: The habit of booking multiple options and cancelling (or simply not showing) normalised during 2020–2022. No-show rates in NZ hospitality are estimated at 5–12%, compared to 2–4% pre-pandemic.

**Occasion-driven dining**: More of the dining-out decision is tied to a specific occasion — birthday, date night, celebration, work event — rather than casual spontaneous dining. This shifts demand toward the weekend and toward experiences rather than routine meals.

**Discovery platform shift**: Google and Instagram have consolidated as the primary discovery channels for NZ diners. Referral-based platforms have maintained relevance but the balance of initial discovery has shifted toward search and social.

## What Is Working for NZ Venues in 2026

The venues outperforming their market share in 2026 share patterns that are consistent enough to be meaningful:

**Direct customer relationships**: Venues with a functioning email list and a habit of communicating with their customers directly are more resilient to platform changes, algorithm shifts, and slow trading periods.

**Deliberate quiet session strategy**: The Friday and Saturday sessions mostly fill themselves in most markets. The businesses with strong weekly revenue have deliberately solved for Monday–Thursday rather than hoping the off-peak trade arrives.

**Correct pricing**: Venues that raised their menu prices to reflect actual 2024–2026 ingredient costs, rather than hoping costs would return to 2022 levels, are operating with correct margins. Those that did not are working harder for less.

**No-show management**: Venues with active no-show protection — pre-authorisation, deposits, clear cancellation policies — are not absorbing the 5–12% booking loss that poorly-managed venues are.

**Platform diversification**: The venues in the most stable position are not dependent on any single discovery or booking channel. They use platforms for new customer acquisition, own the customer relationship afterwards, and build toward greater direct booking over time.

## The Opportunity

The venues closing in 2026 are leaving customers. Some of those customers are looking for a new regular spot. The venues positioned to acquire them are the ones that are easy to find online, have a reputation for quality and consistency, and communicate directly with their existing customers in a way that maintains warmth and presence.

The conditions are hard. They are also sorting the market. The operators who come through this transition in good shape will face a competitive environment that has thinned somewhat, with a customer base that has recalibrated its expectations.
