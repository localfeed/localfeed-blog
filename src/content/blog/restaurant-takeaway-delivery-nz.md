Uber Eats and DoorDash are ubiquitous in NZ cities. Every restaurant gets approached to join. The pitch is straightforward: reach a new customer segment, generate additional revenue, with no upfront cost to the venue.

The actual economics are more complicated. Here is the honest breakdown.

## What the Commission Actually Costs

Delivery platforms typically charge 25–35% commission on the order value, paid by the venue. This is not the delivery fee the customer pays — that is separate. The platform's revenue from the venue is a percentage of every order.

At 30% commission:
- A $45 order generates $13.50 in commission to the platform
- The venue receives $31.50
- The food cost on a $45 order at 30% food cost is $13.50
- The gross on the order before labour and packaging: $18

Compare to a dine-in equivalent:
- A $45 food order delivers $31.50 gross (70% margin) in the restaurant
- No packaging cost, no commission, no delivery timing issue

The dine-in equivalent generates 75% more gross profit on the same order.

## When Delivery Makes Commercial Sense

**Incremental revenue at off-peak times**: If your kitchen is running during lunch with minimal additional labour and delivery orders come in, the incremental gross on those orders — even at thin margin — is additive. The kitchen is already on. The cost is already running.

**Concept fit**: High-volume, lower-price concepts (pizza, burgers, rice dishes) have lower food costs (22–27%) and can sustain delivery commission better than fine dining or food-cost-intensive concepts. A $25 burger at 25% food cost ($6.25) leaves $18.75 after 30% commission ($7.50). Not strong, but workable at volume.

**Genuine new customer reach**: If the delivery platform reaches customers who would genuinely never visit your dine-in venue, it is building a brand in a new segment. If it is primarily cannibalising customers who would have come in and ordered at full margin, it is not additive.

## When Delivery Does Not Make Commercial Sense

**High food cost menus**: A $55 main at 35% food cost has $19.25 in ingredients. After 30% commission ($16.50), you retain $19.25. Your gross is zero. Delivery with high food cost items is often loss-making.

**Premium dining context**: Cuisine that depends on presentation, temperature, and ambiance to deliver its value proposition does not translate to delivery. The customer experience is degraded and the review risk is high.

**When it displaces dine-in orders**: If customers who would have visited your restaurant start ordering delivery because it is convenient, you have exchanged a $31.50 gross dine-in table for an $18 delivery order. This is a net negative.

## Designing Delivery Correctly (If You Do It)

**Create a delivery-specific menu**: Not everything on your dine-in menu travels well or holds its value delivered. A delivery menu with 8–12 items specifically designed to travel well, priced for delivery economics, with lower food costs, is a different product from your dine-in menu.

**Delivery-specific pricing**: Many NZ venues price their delivery menu 15–20% above their dine-in menu to offset commission. This is common, understood by the market, and appropriate. Customers paying delivery platform prices expect to pay more.

**Direct ordering channel**: The commission cost of third-party delivery platforms is the argument for building a direct online ordering channel. A venue that can take 30% of its delivery orders through its own website (at zero commission) rather than through Uber Eats (at 30% commission) significantly improves delivery economics.

## The Alternative: Click-and-Collect

Click-and-collect (online ordering, customer picks up) delivers the revenue without the delivery infrastructure or the high platform commission. Several NZ POS systems (Square, Lightspeed) have built-in online ordering for click-and-collect.

At zero or minimal commission, click-and-collect at a modest volume can be more commercially valuable than Uber Eats at higher volume. The maths is simple: 10 click-and-collect orders at $45 each with zero commission generates the same gross as 14 Uber Eats orders at $45 each at 30% commission.

## The Honest Assessment

Delivery works commercially for a specific set of NZ venues: high-volume, moderate food cost concepts with a genuine incremental revenue opportunity at off-peak times. It does not work well for most fine-dining, high-food-cost, or premium-experience venues.

If you are on delivery platforms and have not run the actual margin analysis, run it. The number of NZ venues doing delivery at neutral or negative margin without realising it is significant.
