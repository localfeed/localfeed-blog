A restaurant booking system is not just reservation management software. It is the infrastructure that determines whether you own your customer relationships, how much each booking actually costs you, and whether your tables fill on your terms or someone else's.

Choosing it correctly is more consequential than most venue operators realise.

## What a Booking System Actually Does (All of Them)

Every booking system, regardless of its marketing positioning, does some combination of:

1. **Discovery**: Helps diners find your venue when they are looking for somewhere to eat
2. **Reservation management**: Takes bookings, manages your table inventory, sends confirmations
3. **Communication**: Sends reminders to diners and notifications to your team
4. **Data collection**: Collects information about the diner and the booking

Where systems differ — significantly — is in who owns the data from step 4, how much they charge for steps 1–3, and what obligations they place on you in exchange for discovery.

## The Three Models Operating in NZ

### Discovery-Discount Platforms

First Table is the clearest example. Discovery is tied to a mandatory discount (50% off food). The diner pays a booking fee to the platform. The venue absorbs the discount. Customer data belongs to the platform.

**Best for**: Filling genuinely empty sessions when discovery is the problem and you can absorb the margin hit.

**Not suitable for**: Building customer relationships, maintaining margin, controlling your offer.

### Commission-Per-Cover Platforms

Dimmi (TheFork) and OpenTable charge the venue a commission per booked diner — typically $1.50–$3 per cover, plus a monthly subscription in some configurations. These platforms have large diner bases and significant discovery power.

**Best for**: Urban venues with high competition where broad platform discovery provides genuine incremental bookings.

**Not suitable for**: Venues where the commission cost is not justified by the bookings generated, or venues prioritising data ownership.

### Flat-Fee and Venue-Controlled Platforms

LocalFeed and certain reservation-only tools (OpenTable's front-of-house reservation tool, Tock, Resy) charge a flat subscription or flat fee without per-cover commission, and give venues control over their offer and customer data.

[LocalFeed](https://localfeed.app) charges $10 per week flat (free until 20 bookings), requires no mandatory discount, and passes all customer contact data to the venue.

**Best for**: Venues that want to control their offer, own their customer data, and not pay commission on volume.

## The Data Ownership Evaluation

For every booking system you evaluate, ask explicitly: does my venue receive the customer's email address and name from every booking?

If the answer is no (or "only if the customer opts in"), you are not building a customer base — you are contributing to the platform's customer base.

Over 12 months of 500 bookings per month: 6,000 customer interactions. The question is whether you own 6,000 contacts or zero.

## The True Cost Comparison

Marketing materials for booking platforms focus on the visible fee. The true cost includes:

| Platform | Visible cost | Hidden cost | Annual cost at 400 bookings/month |
|---|---|---|---|
| First Table | $0 venue fee | 50% food discount (~$45 food gross lost per cover) | ~$216,000 in lost food gross |
| Dimmi ($2/cover) | $2/cover | Monthly subscription | ~$9,600 + subscription |
| LocalFeed | $10/week | None | ~$520 |

The First Table calculation is illustrative at high volume — most venues limit their First Table exposure to a subset of sessions. But the direction is consistent: "free to the venue" platforms are not free. They are paid in margin.

## The No-Show Question

Ask every booking system vendor: what is your no-show policy and how does it protect the venue?

The answers range from robust (pre-authorisation capability, deposit collection, automated reminder sequences, clear cancellation policies built in) to minimal (a confirmation email and then nothing).

No-show protection is particularly important for high-value sessions (Friday and Saturday dinners) and for large group bookings where a no-show is a significant revenue event.

## Recommendation Framework

**New venue, limited budget, need discovery**: Start with LocalFeed (free until 20 bookings) and build your direct booking base from the first customer.

**Established venue, high competition market, need maximum discovery**: Consider Dimmi for broad exposure but limit your exposure and build your direct list simultaneously.

**Venue with strong existing customer base**: A venue-side reservation tool (no third-party discovery) plus your own direct marketing is the most cost-effective configuration.
