When a First Table diner cancels or does not show, what happens to your reserved table and your prepped mise en place? The answer reveals something important about how the platform's accountability structure is designed — and for whom.

## How First Table's Policy Works

First Table's primary accountability mechanism is the booking fee the diner pays upfront ($10–$15 NZD). If a diner cancels within a specified period of the booking, they may forfeit this fee depending on the platform's current terms.

The critical point: **the booking fee goes to First Table, not to the venue.**

When a diner cancels at the last minute or does not show:
- The platform retains the diner's booking fee (which was paid to them, not you)
- The venue receives no compensation for the blocked table, the prepped food, or the rostered staff
- The venue also does not receive customer contact details to rebook the slot

The platform's revenue from the booking is protected by the booking fee. The venue's lost revenue from the no-show is not.

## The Real Cost to the Venue

A First Table no-show creates a compounding problem:

**The direct loss**: The food prepped for the table. At 50% of normal food revenue and 30% food cost, the prepped cost on a table of two at $90 normal food spend is approximately $27 in ingredients that may or may not be salvageable.

**The opportunity cost**: The table was blocked in your system from the time the booking was made. You could not take another booking for that slot because First Table had reserved it. If you had had a direct booking in that slot at full margin, the opportunity cost of the no-show is the full margin of that table.

**The no-show pattern**: If First Table bookings have a structurally higher no-show rate than direct bookings (which some NZ operators report), the platform's fill solution is actually creating a recurring revenue gap.

## What Venues Can Do

**Note your First Table no-show rate**: If you are not tracking no-shows by booking source, start. If First Table bookings no-show at a higher rate than direct bookings, this is a data point worth acting on.

**Communicate the slot is reserved**: Some operators contact First Table bookings directly before the session to confirm attendance. This is not guaranteed contact — you may not have the diner's details — but it can reduce the forgotten booking problem.

**Consider your slot availability**: You control which sessions you open on First Table. If no-shows are a persistent problem in certain slots, close those slots on the platform and rely on your own channels for them.

## The Structural Difference

The reason First Table's no-show accountability is weaker than ideal is structural, not accidental. The platform's accountability mechanism (the booking fee) is designed to protect the platform's revenue, not the venue's.

Booking platforms that are designed with venue interests in mind structure accountability differently: deposits or pre-authorisation go to the venue, or the platform takes an active role in reducing no-shows through reminder sequences and follow-up.

## The Broader Question

The no-show policy is one data point in the broader evaluation of First Table for your venue. It is worth considering alongside the margin impact of the 50% food discount, the data ownership question (no customer contact details), and the habituation risk (diners who return expecting a discount).

None of these are reasons to dismiss the platform entirely. First Table solves a real problem. They are reasons to use it with clear eyes about the limitations and to supplement it with booking channels that offer stronger venue protection.
