The proportion of NZ diners with dietary requirements — coeliac disease, nut allergies, dairy intolerance, vegetarian, vegan, halal — has grown significantly over the past decade. For many restaurant teams, managing these requirements is a source of stress, complexity, and occasional kitchen chaos.

The venues that handle dietary requirements best have made a deliberate operational choice: to build the capability into their systems rather than treating each request as an exception.

## The Commercial Case for Handling It Well

A coeliac diner who finds a restaurant that genuinely handles their requirement safely and without drama becomes one of the most loyal customers in hospitality. The options available to them are limited. The social proof they create — "this place actually gets it, you can eat there safely" — within their community is powerful.

The same applies to the vegan diner, the nut allergy parent choosing for a family, the halal-observing guest. Each underserved segment represents diners who are actively looking for venues that make them feel welcome rather than inconvenienced.

Beyond loyalty: a table of six where one person has coeliac disease and the rest do not will choose a restaurant that confidently handles the coeliac — for every person at that table, for every future booking.

## The Allergy vs Preference Distinction

The most important operational distinction in dietary management:

**Allergy or medical requirement**: Coeliac, nut allergy, severe dairy allergy. These require strict cross-contamination protocols. Getting this wrong has health consequences. Take these with absolute seriousness.

**Dietary preference**: Plant-based eating, avoiding meat, avoiding gluten by preference. These require menu flexibility but not the same cross-contamination vigilance as a medical requirement.

Your team must understand the difference and ask the right question when a dietary requirement is noted. "Is this an allergy or intolerance, or a preference?" is a direct question that enables the right response.

## Building the Kitchen System

**Label your menu clearly**: Which items are gluten-free, which are vegan, which contain common allergens. Clear menu labelling reduces the number of verbal requests your team has to manage and speeds up the ordering process.

**Create a subset of genuinely safe items**: Rather than attempting to modify every item on your menu for every dietary requirement, identify five to eight items that can be prepared safely for common requirements without modification complexity. Make these your go-to recommendations.

**Establish cross-contamination protocols**: Separate boards, separate utensils, separate preparation surfaces, communication to the kitchen about which orders require strict protocols. This requires training, not just intention.

**Communication between floor and kitchen**: The order ticket for a coeliac diner should be marked at every point in the system — on the dupe, called through, verbally confirmed. The failure point in dietary management is almost always a communication gap between floor and kitchen.

## What to Say When You Cannot Accommodate

Sometimes the honest answer is: "We cannot safely accommodate that requirement with our current menu." This is better than attempting to accommodate it poorly and causing harm or a bad experience.

Train your team to say it confidently and to suggest alternatives — a nearby venue that handles that requirement well, or items on your menu that are adjacent to what the diner wanted.

A venue that is honest about what it cannot do safely builds more trust than a venue that says yes to everything and delivers inconsistently.

## Dietary Requirements and Menu Design

When redesigning your menu seasonally, include a dietary review: which items can be made vegan with minimal modification? Which items can be made gluten-free with a small component swap? Which items should be marked as containing the most common allergens?

A seasonal menu that has five confidently vegan items and three clearly marked gluten-free items is better than a menu with no marking and a "we can accommodate" policy that your team implements differently on every table.

## Marketing Your Dietary Capability

If your venue handles dietary requirements genuinely well, communicate it. Your Google Business Profile can mention dietary options in the description. Your website should have a dietary requirements note. Your Instagram can include content specifically about how you handle coeliac or vegan requirements.

NZ diners with dietary requirements search specifically for venues that can accommodate them. Being findable in that search is new customer acquisition at zero cost.
