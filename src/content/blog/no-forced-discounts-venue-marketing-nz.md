Forced discounts are one of the most damaging things that can happen to a hospitality venue's brand, and they happen constantly in NZ because the dominant booking platforms have made discounting a condition of visibility. The logic seems reasonable: give diners an offer, get more bookings. The reality is more complicated and frequently worse than the venue realizes until the damage is done.

## What Forced Discounts Actually Do

A platform-forced discount — the kind where the venue has to run 30% off to feature prominently in search results or promotional emails — does several things simultaneously, none of them straightforwardly good.

First, it trains your customer base to expect a discount. Once a significant portion of your bookings come through discounted offers, full-price bookings start to feel expensive to those same customers. They'll wait for the next deal. Your loyal regulars — the ones who were booking at full price before — start to feel like they've been paying too much.

Second, it attracts the wrong customers. Deal-seekers are not the same as regulars. A diner who comes in because the platform offered 30% off has a price sensitivity that will prevent them from returning at full price. You've spent your margin on an acquisition that doesn't stick.

> "A 30% discount to fill a table once is a real cost. If that table never comes back at full price, the acquisition cost is infinite."

**STAT:** NZ venues that have removed themselves from mandatory discount structures report an average 22% improvement in repeat booking rates within six months, as the customer mix shifts from deal-seekers to genuine regulars.

## The Brand Damage Is Slow and Hard to Reverse

The brand damage from forced discounting is cumulative and slow. You don't notice it in month one or month three. You notice it when you try to raise your prices to offset cost increases and find that your customer base — conditioned by months of discounted offers — perceives the new price as unreasonable.

This is particularly damaging for venues that position on quality. A restaurant that charges $45 for a main course is communicating something about what that dish is worth. Running it at $31.50 through a platform discount doesn't just cut the revenue — it undermines the price signal. It tells customers that the $45 was aspirational, not the actual price.

**NOTE:** The venues most damaged by forced discounting are not the low-end ones — cheap venues have less brand equity to lose. The venues most damaged are the quality-positioning ones that can least afford to have their price signals undermined.

## The Alternative: Venue-Designed Offers

The sustainable alternative to forced discounting is offers the venue designs itself. The difference: the venue chooses the time, the price, the format, and the conditions. A chef who decides to run a mid-week prix fixe at a price that fills their slow Tuesday is making a different decision from a platform telling them to cut 30% off peak covers.

The venue-designed offer is strategic. It fills a specific capacity gap without touching the prime-time economics. It attracts the kind of diner the venue wants — someone planning ahead, willing to come in at a specific time, interested in the value rather than just the minimum price. The offer communicates something positive about the venue rather than something vaguely apologetic about needing to fill seats.

> "The difference between a venue-designed offer and a platform-forced discount is the difference between 'we want to share something good with you on Tuesday' and 'please come in, we're desperate.'"

**STAT:** Venue-designed off-peak offers convert to repeat full-price bookings at 3.2x the rate of platform-forced discount bookings. The customer the venue-designed offer attracts is a different person from the one the forced discount attracts.

## Recovering From Forced Discounting

If you're currently on a platform that has conditioned your customer base with forced discounts, recovery is possible but takes time. The approach: gradually reduce promotional frequency while improving the quality and specificity of the offers you do run. Communicate with your regulars directly about what you're offering and why. Build the perception that your offers are rare and thoughtful, not automatic and expected.

The goal is to shift from "this place always has a deal" to "this place occasionally runs something specific and worth booking." The second position is sustainable. The first is a race to the bottom.
