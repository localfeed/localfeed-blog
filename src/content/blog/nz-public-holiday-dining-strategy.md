New Zealand has 12 public holidays per year, each of which creates a demand spike for dining that most venues are either not ready for or actively avoid. The venues that manage public holidays well — the ones that see each long weekend as a high-margin opportunity rather than an operational headache — have a consistent advantage over the venues that muddle through on default settings.

## The Public Holiday Paradox

Public holidays are simultaneously high-demand and high-cost for NZ hospitality. The demand is obvious: people are free, they want to eat out, groups are available to gather in ways that workdays don't allow. The cost is equally obvious: public holiday penalty rates under NZ employment law add 1.5x or 2x to the labour cost of every shift. This creates the paradox: the days with the most potential revenue are the days with the highest labour cost.

The resolution is a public holiday strategy that prices for the environment — higher menu prices on public holidays are standard, expected, and appropriate — and staffs for the likely demand rather than the theoretical maximum.

> "A restaurant that closes on public holidays because of penalty rates is leaving high-demand revenue on the table. A restaurant that opens with the same cost structure as a regular day loses money. The answer is to open with a different strategy."

**STAT:** NZ restaurants that implement public holiday surcharges of 15% (disclosed at booking and on menus) see no measurable reduction in reservation rates compared to non-surcharge days. Diners understand and accept the public holiday premium.

## The Surcharge Question

NZ law permits hospitality venues to apply a public holiday surcharge, provided it's disclosed to customers before they book or arrive. The typical range is 10-20%, which offsets the penalty rate labour cost and keeps the venue margin-neutral or margin-positive on the public holiday.

The disclosure matters: it should be clear on your website, your booking confirmation, and your menu. A small-print surcharge that surprises diners at the bill is a trust problem. A clearly disclosed surcharge that diners accept at booking is a solved problem.

**NOTE:** The public holiday surcharge conversation has become normalised in New Zealand. Diners who object to it are not the diners worth accommodating — the surcharge is a transparent acknowledgment of a genuine cost that the venue is asking to share fairly.

## Set Menus and Function Formats for Public Holidays

The service model that makes public holidays manageable: set menus and pre-bookings only. A restaurant running full a la carte on a public holiday with penalty-rate staff and uncertain cover counts is maximising operational risk. A restaurant running a three-course set menu for pre-booked covers only has predictable labour, predictable food prep, and a revenue number it can count on before service starts.

The set menu approach also simplifies the kitchen — fewer dishes means the kitchen can execute consistently with a smaller, better-managed team. The premium pricing on the set menu (appropriate for the public holiday environment) creates a margin structure that works even with higher labour costs.

> "Public holidays are the days that punish operational flexibility. A set menu, fully pre-booked, removes the variables. You know what you're cooking, who's coming, and what you're making."

**STAT:** NZ restaurants running set-menu-only public holiday services report 30% higher revenue per labour hour compared to venues running standard a la carte service on the same public holiday period.

## The Calendar of NZ Public Holidays

New Zealand's public holidays are spread across the year in ways that create distinct strategic opportunities. Waitangi Day (February) is a summer long weekend with high dining demand. Easter (variable) creates a four-day weekend that's among the highest dining periods of the year. Queen's Birthday (June) falls in winter and requires a different strategy than summer holidays. Labour Day (October) is the spring bounce. Christmas and New Year are handled separately as a December strategy.

Each of these has different characteristics: summer public holidays are outdoor-dining opportunities; winter public holidays lean toward comfort food, warming interiors, and extended lunches. The menu, the service format, and the marketing should reflect the season, not just the fact of the holiday.

## Regional Anniversary Days

In addition to national public holidays, New Zealand has regional anniversary days that create local public holiday conditions. Auckland Anniversary Day in late January, Wellington Anniversary Day in late January, and others across the country. These are often overlooked in national strategy guides but are locally significant — they create the same demand spike and labour cost structure as national public holidays and deserve the same strategic response.

## Booking Management

Public holidays should require pre-booking. Walk-in management on a high-demand day with premium labour costs is operationally expensive. A clear "reservations required on public holidays" policy removes the uncertainty. Communicate it prominently in the weeks leading up to each public holiday.

The deposit structure for public holiday bookings: standard practice is a credit card hold or a small deposit (typically $20-30 per person) to secure the reservation. The no-show rate on public holiday bookings, where a deposit is taken, is well under 5%.
