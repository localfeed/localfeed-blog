Drinks margin is the most forgiving margin in hospitality. Wine at 22–30% cost, cocktails at 18–24%, beer at 20–28% — versus food at 28–35%. A table that orders two glasses of wine adds $25–$40 in gross profit with minimal additional labour. A table that drinks water adds zero.

Building a wine list that your team understands and your diners order from is one of the most commercially valuable things a NZ restaurant operator can do.

## The Design Principle: Approachable, Not Encyclopaedic

The most common mistake in NZ restaurant wine lists is length. A list of 120 wines requires the customer to make a decision they are not confident making, often results in ordering the safest (and lowest-margin) option, and overwhelms floor staff who cannot speak confidently to the full range.

A list of 20–35 wines, curated by someone who knows them, priced intelligently, and described with enough specificity that a customer can self-select, outsells a 120-bottle list in most casual and mid-market NZ venues.

The principle: fewer wines, better-known by the team, sold with more confidence.

## What the List Should Include

**Wines by the glass (6–10 options)**: The primary sell opportunity for most tables. Cover white, red, rosé, and sparkling at minimum. Include at least one premium by-the-glass option that enables upselling without requiring a full bottle commitment.

**Bottles across a price range**: A clear entry (under $55), mid-range ($55–$90), and premium tier ($90+). The mid-range is where most tables land — make it the most curated and confident part of your list.

**NZ wines prominently**: NZ diners have pride in local wine. An NZ-first or NZ-forward list has genuine commercial and cultural resonance, particularly with local regulars and tourists who want an authentic Aotearoa experience.

**At least one natural or skin-contact option**: The natural wine segment continues to grow among food-engaged NZ diners, particularly in urban markets. Having one well-chosen option acknowledges this without committing the list to a direction that does not suit your venue's style.

## Pricing Your Wine List

**By the glass**: Target 22–28% cost. At $14 glass retail and a 25% cost target, your glass price should be $56/bottle equivalent, or roughly $14 per glass for a $14 cost-per-glass wine.

More practically: if you pay $18/bottle wholesale for a house white and pour five glasses per bottle, your cost per glass is $3.60. At 25% cost target, sell at $14.40 — so $14–$15 per glass.

**Bottles**: A common NZ restaurant markup is 3–3.5x wholesale cost, reducing to 2.5x for premium bottles above $100 retail to maintain price relativity with the retail market.

The benchmark question to ask: is our most expensive bottle on the list actually ordered? If a $250 bottle has never moved in six months, it is anchoring the list psychologically but not commercially. Consider replacing it with something at $130–$150 that sells.

## Training the Floor Team to Sell Wine

A floor team that can speak confidently to three or four wines on the list — their flavour profile, what food they pair with, why they are worth ordering — sells significantly more wine than a team that defaults to "our house white is $12."

The investment: a monthly team tasting where the chef, the floor manager, and the floor team taste three or four wines currently on the list. Fifteen minutes. No formal training required. Just familiarity.

The result: your team can say "the [producer] pinot gris is really good with the fish — it has this sort of stone fruit thing that works nicely against the sauce." That sells wine. "Would you like to see the wine list?" does not.

## Wine as an Event Vehicle

The single best commercial use of your wine list is as the foundation for events. A winery dinner, a wine tasting, a monthly "winemaker's table" — these events:

- Generate advance ticket revenue
- Create content for email and social
- Bring the winery's audience to your venue
- Fill sessions that might otherwise be quiet
- Build a reputation for wine knowledge that attracts the wine-interested segment

NZ's wine industry is full of small producers who are interested in venue partnerships. The outreach is not complicated: email three wineries whose wines you already use, explain that you run occasional wine dinners for 20–30 guests, and ask whether they would be interested in collaborating.
