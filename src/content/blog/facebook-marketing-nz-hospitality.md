Organic reach on Facebook business pages is approximately 5–6% in 2026. For a page with 2,000 followers, that means 100–120 people see an average post. Most of those are existing customers who would have come in anyway. The era of Facebook as a free marketing channel for NZ restaurants is over. That does not mean Facebook is useless — it means you need to use it differently.

## Where Facebook still works for NZ hospo

**Local community Facebook groups** are the most valuable Facebook channel available to NZ venue owners, and most are not using them.

Groups like "Hamilton East Locals," "Ponsonby Community Group," "Tauranga Foodies," and suburb-specific Facebook groups in every NZ city are active, trusted, and genuinely local. When a member posts "where should I take my mum for brunch in Newmarket this weekend?" and a regular from your cafe responds with a genuine recommendation, that interaction reaches hundreds of relevant local people at zero cost.

The approach is participation, not promotion. Join the three or four groups most relevant to your venue's neighbourhood. Answer questions about local dining when they arise. Post genuinely useful content occasionally: "our kitchen is making a batch of beef and mushroom pies this Saturday, first come first served." Not an ad. A useful piece of local information.

**STAT:** 12x · Engagement rate difference between a genuine post in a local Facebook community group versus a promotional post on a business page. Community groups have trust. Business page posts are treated as ads.

## The Facebook tools that still have genuine value

**Facebook Events**: for ticketed dinners, tasting events, chef's tables, or any event with a specific date and limited capacity, Facebook Events still drives meaningful attendance for NZ venues. Local diners browse Events actively. A well-described event with a specific location, clear booking mechanism, and updated RSVP count gets genuine organic reach that a standard page post does not.

**Facebook Messenger**: customer service and booking enquiries through Messenger are common in NZ hospo. Responding quickly to Messenger messages signals active business to Facebook's algorithm and to prospective customers. A response time badge under an hour is visible on your page and influences booking decisions.

**Facebook Page as a directory listing**: even if you never post, your Facebook page functions as a local directory entry that appears in searches. Keep your hours, address, phone, and menu link current. Some NZ diners use Facebook specifically to find opening hours and contact information.

> Your Facebook page's value in 2026 is less as a content channel and more as a local directory entry and event listing platform. Treat it accordingly.

## What is not worth your time on Facebook

**Posting regularly to your business page feed**: the reach is too low to justify the effort unless you are boosting every post. Use the time elsewhere.

**Boosting posts to a broad NZ audience**: a $50 boost to "Auckland women aged 25–45 who like food" is reaching people who are not near your venue. If you boost, set the audience radius to 5–10km from your address. Budget for boosting is often better spent on Google Ads if you have any digital ad budget.

**Facebook competitions and giveaways**: "tag a friend to win" competitions have been used by NZ hospo for years. They generate follower counts, not customers. The people who enter competitions for a free breakfast are not necessarily the people who will pay for a regular breakfast. The conversion from competition entry to paying regular is low.

**NOTE:** If you are currently spending more than 30 minutes per week on Facebook content for your NZ venue and you have no evidence it is generating covers, reallocate that time to Google review responses and local community group participation. The return will be higher.

## The Facebook audience that is still worth reaching

The one demographic where Facebook remains genuinely strong for NZ hospitality: the 40+ local diner. This audience uses Facebook regularly, trusts local recommendations in community groups, and is less likely to be on Instagram or TikTok.

For a pub in Whanganui, a cafe in Palmerston North, or a bistro in Hastings where the core audience skews older and local, a Facebook community group strategy has more return than an Instagram strategy. Know your audience and use the platform they use.

For younger urban audiences in Auckland, Wellington, and Christchurch, Instagram and TikTok are more effective. For mixed-age suburban and provincial NZ audiences, Facebook community groups remain the most trusted local recommendation channel.

Facebook for NZ hospitality in 2026 is a community group play and a directory maintenance task, not a content channel. Stop posting to your page feed and start participating in the local groups where your actual customers are having conversations. That is where the value lives.
