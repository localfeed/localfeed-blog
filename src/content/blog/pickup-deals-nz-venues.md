Pickup deals are one of the cleaner off-peak revenue strategies available to NZ hospitality venues. No table covers to manage, no service labour cost at the same ratio as dine-in, no front-of-house allocation for the slot. A pickup customer pays for the food, picks it up, and leaves. Done correctly, it fills production capacity without the overhead of the full dining experience.

## Why Pickup Deals Make Economic Sense

The economics of a pickup deal are different from a dine-in cover in a specific and useful way. The labour cost of a pickup is lower. You're not rostering front-of-house for the interaction in the same way. The table isn't occupied for 90 minutes. The kitchen is using capacity that exists anyway — the slow Tuesday afternoon prep time, the gap between lunch and dinner service.

A pickup deal priced correctly — not so low that it undercuts the dine-in economics, not so high that there's no value — generates margin that would otherwise be zero. The choice isn't between a pickup deal and a dine-in cover. It's between a pickup deal and nothing.

> "A pickup at $18 in your slow Tuesday afternoon slot costs you roughly $7 in food and $2 in packaging and handling. The $9 that's left over is margin you didn't have."

**STAT:** NZ hospitality venues that implemented structured pickup programmes post-COVID retained an average of 65% of their takeaway volume when dining rooms reopened. The channel is additive, not substitutive — customers who pickup are different from customers who dine in.

## What Works as a Pickup Deal

Not everything translates to pickup. The dishes that work are the ones that travel well and maintain their quality for the 10-20 minutes between kitchen and consumption: grain bowls, soups, sandwiches, cold plates, pastry, high-quality prepared meals. The dishes that don't work are the ones that require the dining room environment to be good — a perfectly plated fine dining dish that collapses in a container isn't a pickup deal, it's a disappointment.

The venues that run pickup deals well have created a specific pickup menu rather than offering the full dine-in menu for collection. The pickup menu is designed around travel resilience, container economics, and preparation speed. It's a different product, priced appropriately, that serves a specific customer need.

**NOTE:** The biggest mistake venues make with pickup is offering a bad version of the dine-in experience in a container. The second biggest is not thinking about the packaging. A $20 grain bowl in a flimsy disposable container is a different product from the same bowl in a well-sealed, beautiful container. The packaging is part of the experience.

## The Off-Peak Application

Pickup deals are most powerful when used to fill specific off-peak slots. The slow Tuesday afternoon. The post-lunch gap from 2-4pm. The pre-dinner window from 4-5:30pm where the kitchen is in transition. These are times when there's production capacity available and no competing dine-in demand.

A cafe that does a 3pm pickup deal for end-of-day baking is using production that would otherwise be wasted. A restaurant that offers a Friday noon pickup of their weekly special is capturing customers who want restaurant-quality food but are eating at their desk. The slot specificity is important — a general "we do pickup" is less compelling than "pickup Friday noon only, chef's special, $22, 20 available."

> "A pickup deal that's limited and specific creates demand through scarcity. 'Twenty available, Friday only' is a different offer from 'always available, whenever.'"

**STAT:** Limited-availability pickup deals with specific slots and quantities sell out at 3.5x the rate of open-ended pickup offers. The constraint creates urgency that drives booking and reduces the operational uncertainty of not knowing how many to prepare.

## The Loyalty Angle

Pickup customers, done right, become regulars. The person who picks up your Friday noon special three weeks in a row is building a relationship with your kitchen without the friction of booking a table. When they eventually come in for a proper dinner, they're not a new customer — they're someone who already trusts your food.

The pickup channel is a lower-commitment entry point into a venue relationship. For customers who are curious about a new place but not yet ready to commit to a sit-down meal, it's the right introduction.
