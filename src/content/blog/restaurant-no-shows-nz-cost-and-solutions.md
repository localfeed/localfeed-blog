You rostered three extra staff. You ordered in. You prepped the mise en place for a full Friday. Then four tables did not show, and by 8pm you were looking at a floor that was 40% full and a payroll that was 100% committed.

No-shows are not an occasional annoyance. They are a structural problem in NZ hospitality, and the industry has been absorbing the cost quietly for years.

## What a No-Show Actually Costs

The visible cost is the food that was prepped and not sold. The less visible costs are larger.

A table of four at a typical NZ restaurant represents around $200-$260 in food and drinks revenue. The gross contribution of that table, after food cost, is roughly $140-$180. When the table does not show, that contribution is zero. The prepped food may be salvageable or it may not be.

But the real cost is the compounding effect:

- The table was blocked in the booking system, preventing another booking from filling that slot
- Staff were rostered to cover the session based on expected covers
- The kitchen prepped based on covers expected, not covers arrived
- If the no-show happens repeatedly, staff begin to pace differently, prep less confidently, and the service quality for the tables that did arrive can suffer

Industry estimates in NZ suggest no-shows run at 5-12% of all restaurant bookings across the country. At a venue doing 600 covers per month, that is 30-70 covers lost before service begins. At $35-$45 gross contribution per cover, that is $1,050-$3,150 in lost monthly gross profit.

That is not a rounding error. That is a staff member's monthly wages.

## Why No-Shows Have Gotten Worse

Several factors are converging:

**Zero-consequence booking systems.** When there is no card on file, no deposit, and no reminder, cancelling or simply not showing up has no cost for the diner. They might have booked three restaurants for the same night and picked the one that felt right on the day. Your table was collateral.

**Platform-facilitated forgetting.** Some booking platforms send one confirmation and no follow-up. A booking made two weeks out is easily forgotten without a 24-hour reminder.

**Post-pandemic expectations.** The habit of cancelling at short notice became normalised during 2020-2022 for obvious reasons. Some of that behaviour has stuck even as venues returned to normal.

## The Most Effective Solutions

### Pre-authorisation and deposits

Taking a credit card at booking, with a clear policy that a fee is charged for no-shows within a specified window (24-48 hours), reduces no-shows significantly. The threshold matters: venues that charge a clear, reasonable no-show fee, typically $20-$30 per person, and communicate it clearly at booking, report significantly lower no-show rates than venues with no policy.

The communication is as important as the fee. Diners who agree to a cancellation policy at booking time feel accountable in a way that a bare booking confirmation does not create.

### Reminder sequences

A booking confirmation followed by a reminder 48 hours out and another the morning of the reservation captures most would-be no-shows before they happen. If someone was going to forget, the reminder gives them the opportunity to cancel with enough notice for you to rebook the slot.

Most no-shows are not malicious. They are absent-minded. A reminder turns an absent-minded no-show into a managed cancellation.

### Smaller waitlists

Maintaining an active waitlist for popular sessions and filling cancelled slots quickly reduces the revenue impact even when a no-show does occur. If a table cancels at noon for a 7pm sitting and you can fill it from the waitlist by 3pm, the cost of that cancellation is minimal.

### Platform choice

Not all booking platforms build in the same accountability mechanisms. Platforms that allow pre-authorisation, that send robust reminder sequences, and that give venues tools to manage cancellation policies directly are meaningfully different from those that do not.

## The Conversation Worth Having With Your Team

No-show management is not just a technology problem. It is a culture and communication problem.

Your front-of-house team should know the cancellation policy and be able to explain it clearly when asked. Your booking confirmation copy should state the policy plainly, not buried in fine print. Your phone manner when taking reservations should include a clear, friendly mention of the cancellation window.

"Just so you know, we ask for at least 24 hours notice if plans change. Is that all good with you?"

Most people say yes. And then they actually honour it.

The venues in NZ that have the lowest no-show rates are not the ones with the harshest policies. They are the ones with the clearest policies, communicated earliest, with a genuine follow-through when the policy is not met.
