A NZ cafe with 30 seats and peak-period bookings does not need a $150/month enterprise reservation system. A 60-seat restaurant running events and private dining probably does. The right reservation tool is a function of your venue's volume, complexity, and what you need from the data afterwards — not a function of what a platform's sales rep tells you that you need.

## What a small NZ venue actually needs from a reservation system

The core requirements for most small NZ venues are simpler than the product demos suggest:

**Availability management**: a real-time view of which tables or time slots are available, so the team can confirm bookings without double-booking.

**Customer contact capture**: name, phone, email, party size, any dietary notes. These are the fields you need for every booking. Everything else is optional.

**Confirmation and reminder communication**: an automated email or text confirmation when the booking is made, and an automated reminder 24 hours before. This alone reduces no-shows by 30–50%.

**A mobile view for the team**: the floor team needs to see the booking sheet on the floor during service. A printed sheet works. A phone view of the system works better.

**The customer contact post-visit**: the email address from the booking is an asset. A system that captures it and lets you export it for marketing is significantly more valuable than one that processes the booking and discards the contact.

Most small NZ venues need exactly these five things. Many systems offer 40 features beyond these at significant cost. Before signing up for features you will not use, establish which five you need and find the option that delivers them at the lowest total cost.

**STAT:** $1,800/year · Subscription cost for a mid-tier NZ restaurant reservation system with full feature set. For a 30-seat cafe doing 20 bookings per week, this is $1.73 per booking. A free tool that captures the contact details and sends a confirmation might serve the need for $0.

## The options available to NZ small venues in 2026

**Google Reserve / Book with Google**: integrates directly with Google Business Profile, allows bookings from the Google Maps result. Free to the venue. Limited functionality. Suitable for venues with simple scheduling needs and low booking volume.

**OpenTable**: well-known, strong diner discovery network, meaningful monthly subscription cost ($250+/month for full-feature). Better suited to higher-volume, higher-ticket restaurants than to small NZ cafes.

**ResDiary**: widely used in NZ hospo, strong reporting, reasonable pricing at lower tiers. A good mid-range option for venues with more than 40 seats and significant booking volume.

**Tock**: strong for venues doing timed seatings, events, and pre-paid experiences. Less suitable for casual booking-optional venues.

**A simple Google Form or Typeform linked to a Google Sheet**: for a very small venue doing under 10 bookings per week, a well-designed form that captures the five essential fields and triggers an automated confirmation email (via Gmail or Zapier) costs nothing and does the job. Scale to a paid system when the volume justifies it.

**LocalFeed for off-peak bookings**: a [LocalFeed](https://localfeed.co.nz) listing is not a full reservation system replacement, but for the specific use case of filling off-peak slots with local diners, it is a lower-cost alternative to a third-party booking platform. The booking includes the customer contact. No commission on the revenue.

> A reservation system that is too complex for the team to use correctly during service is worse than a simple Google Sheet managed by a disciplined floor manager. Complexity that the team ignores is not a feature.

## When to upgrade your reservation system

The signals that your current system is too basic for your operation:

- Double-bookings happening more than once per month
- The team spending more than 30 minutes per day managing bookings manually
- Booking data not being used for any marketing or follow-up activity
- No-show rate above 15% with no systematic reminder process
- Private dining and events being managed on paper while the main room is digital

Any two of these signals together indicate the current system is holding back the operation. The upgrade is worth the cost when the problems it solves are costing you more than the subscription.

The signals that your current system is more than you need:

- Most features are unused after three months of having the system
- The monthly subscription is more than 0.5% of your monthly revenue
- The team frequently defaults to taking phone bookings and logging them manually because the system is easier to bypass than to use
- You are on a month-to-month arrangement and the contract lock-in is the main reason you have not cancelled

**NOTE:** Review your reservation system subscription against your actual usage every six months. If you are using less than half the features you are paying for, downgrade or switch. The savings fund something the operation actually needs.

## The customer data question

The most important long-term question about any NZ reservation system is: who owns the customer data?

A reservation tool that processes bookings and holds the customer database on its servers puts you in a dependency — if you leave the system, you may lose access to your booking history and customer contact list.

The better arrangement is a system that allows full data export at any time, in a standard format, so your customer list is genuinely yours. Before signing any reservation system contract for your NZ venue, ask: "Can I export my full customer contact database at any time? What format does the export come in?" The answer determines whether the customers you are acquiring through bookings belong to you or to the platform.

**FACT:** Zero commission on food revenue. $10/week after 20 bookings. 75% of no-show fees go to the venue.

The right reservation system for a NZ small venue is the simplest one that reliably captures the booking, confirms it automatically, reminds the customer, and gives you the contact for follow-up marketing. Do not pay for complexity you will not use. Scale when the volume and complexity genuinely require it.
