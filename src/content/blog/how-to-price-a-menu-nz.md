Most NZ venue operators price their menus by looking at what the place down the road charges and adjusting slightly. Some use a simple food cost multiple — if the dish costs $8 to make, charge $24 (a 3x markup). Neither approach produces a menu that optimises revenue, sustains the operation, and reflects what the market will pay. Here is a framework that does.

## Starting with the target food cost percentage, not the markup

The correct starting point for menu pricing is your target food cost percentage — the proportion of each dish's selling price that goes to ingredients. For most NZ venues, the target is 28%–35%.

Working from a target food cost percentage:

1. **Calculate the precise ingredient cost per portion**: every component, weighed and costed at your actual purchase price, including yield loss (a 1kg piece of beef at $28/kg with 70% yield costs $40/kg usable, not $28)
2. **Divide by the target food cost percentage**: an ingredient cost of $9.50 at a 30% target gives you $31.67 ($9.50 ÷ 0.30)
3. **Round to a market-appropriate price point**: $31.67 becomes $32, or $33 if the dish positioning supports it, or $29 if the market ceiling requires it

This gives you a price floor — the minimum price at which the dish sustains your target food cost. From there, market factors, positioning, and the dish's role in the menu determine the final price.

**STAT:** 6% · The impact on annual gross profit of a 2-percentage-point improvement in food cost across a NZ restaurant doing $600,000 in food revenue. Menu pricing that is calibrated to a target food cost percentage rather than a rough markup compounds this improvement across the whole menu.

## The market ceiling you cannot price above

Your food cost percentage target determines the floor. Your market position determines the ceiling.

The ceiling is the price above which your target customer switches to an alternative. It is not the same for a Queenstown fine dining restaurant and a Hamilton suburban cafe. It is not fixed — it moves with economic conditions, with what your competitors are doing, and with how your positioning is perceived.

To establish the ceiling: look at your two or three most direct local competitors. What are they charging for similar dishes? What is the top of their price range for your category? That is a ceiling signal, not a ceiling rule — you can price above it if your positioning justifies it, below it if your positioning is value-led.

The practical test: if a dish is routinely returned or if customers comment on the price unprompted, the price is at or above the ceiling for your market. If every dish sells without comment, the prices may be sustainable or they may be below the ceiling — the latter means you are leaving margin on the table.

> Pricing is not a one-time decision. It is an ongoing read of what the market is telling you. The menu that was well-priced in January may need adjustment by August when your ingredient costs have moved.

## The menu engineering principle: not every dish should be priced the same way

Different dishes on your menu play different roles. Understanding those roles shapes how you price them.

**Stars**: high-margin, high-popularity dishes. Price these at the higher end of what the market will accept. They are doing the work of your P&L.

**Plowhorses**: high-popularity, lower-margin dishes. These are often the dishes you cannot remove without losing customers, but they are pulling your food cost percentage up. The response is either to reduce the portion cost (without degrading the dish) or to engineer the menu so they are presented alongside higher-margin options.

**Puzzles**: high-margin, lower-popularity dishes. Often priced too high or not promoted effectively. These dishes should be pushed — mentioned by staff, positioned prominently on the menu, featured in photography.

**Dogs**: low-margin, low-popularity. Remove them. They are consuming kitchen complexity and generating nothing. Every dish that requires specific ingredients and generates below-average margin for below-average order frequency is a cost you do not need.

**NOTE:** Go through your current menu and categorise every dish into these four groups. The process will reveal at least two or three dishes you should either reprice or remove. Do it quarterly.

## The NZ-specific pricing challenges in 2026

NZ food costs have increased significantly in the 2023–2026 period across produce, protein, and dairy. The venues that adjusted their pricing to reflect these cost increases have maintained their margins. The ones that absorbed the increases to avoid customer pushback are running lower margins than they need to be.

The conversation with diners about price increases is uncomfortable. It is also less damaging than most venue owners fear. A well-communicated price change, delivered with transparency about the cost environment, is generally accepted by NZ diners who have experienced the same increases in their own household grocery bills.

How to communicate a price increase without losing customers:

- Change the menu at a natural reset point (seasonal menu update, new print run)
- Do not announce the increase or apologise for it — simply have the new prices on the menu
- If asked directly, be honest: "Our supplier costs have moved significantly and we have adjusted to reflect that"

The venues that make no adjustment accumulate a pricing gap that becomes harder to close the longer it is left. A $2 increase in August is easier than a $6 increase needed by March because three rounds of increases were avoided.

Menu pricing is one of the highest-leverage decisions a NZ venue operator makes. Getting it wrong — pricing too low because competitors set a floor, or pricing without calculating actual ingredient costs — compounds across every cover you serve. Getting it right, and reviewing it regularly as costs move, is the difference between a venue sustaining its margin and one that quietly erodes it over 24 months.
