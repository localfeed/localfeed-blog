The gap between running a good event and running a profitable event is often just marketing. NZ venues with excellent events that regularly struggle to fill them share a common problem: they treat event promotion as an afterthought rather than as an operational discipline with its own timeline and toolkit. Here is the complete guide to marketing NZ venue events in a way that fills them consistently.

## The Email List Is Everything

I'll say this once and make it the loudest thing in this guide: your email list is your most important event marketing tool. Not Instagram. Not Facebook. Not a listing on an events platform. The email list of people who have been to your venue, liked it, and given you permission to contact them is the most direct path to a sold-out event.

If you don't have an email list, building one is the first action item. Capture emails at booking. Offer an incentive if necessary. Import contacts from your booking platform. Ask regulars directly. A list of 300 engaged locals will fill a 30-person event faster than 3,000 Instagram followers.

> "I've watched venues with 15,000 Instagram followers struggle to fill a 20-person wine dinner. The followers weren't the audience. The regulars with their email addresses were."

**STAT:** Email marketing for hospitality events in NZ generates open rates of 35-45% among engaged subscriber lists. Conversion from open to booking averages 8-12%. A 300-person list generates 24-36 bookings from a single well-written email — enough to fill most intimate events.

## The Announcement Sequence

Event marketing works best as a sequence, not a single announcement. The structure that consistently performs well:

Week 1: Email to your list (insiders first, limited availability communicated). Week 2: Social media announcement with booking link. Week 3: Reminder post with social proof (how many seats remain). Week 4: Final call with specific urgency. Each communication should say something slightly different and add information rather than repeating the same message.

**NOTE:** The "seats remaining" communication is the most effective urgency signal in event marketing. "Only 6 seats left" outperforms "book now" as a call to action by a significant margin. Diners respond to specific scarcity, not generic urgency.

## Social Media That Actually Works for Events

Most venue social media is too general to drive event bookings. "We're running a wine dinner next month — stay tuned!" generates interest but not bookings. What works: specific, visual, story-led content.

A post that shows the winemaker you're hosting, the specific wines you're opening, and the menu you're designing around them — with a link to book and a limited-availability message — is a different communication from a generic announcement. It tells people exactly what they'll get and creates a specific reason to act.

Stories (Instagram and Facebook) are more effective than feed posts for event promotion because they create a sense of currency and immediacy. Use them for countdowns, behind-the-scenes prep, and "day before" reminders.

> "Event social media should make people feel like they're missing something specific if they don't book. Not vaguely interested. Actually concerned they'll miss out."

**STAT:** Event posts that include specific menu details and wine or experience descriptions generate 4x more booking link clicks than generic event announcements on NZ venue social media accounts.

## Partnership Promotion

The events that market themselves most effectively often do so through partnerships. A wine dinner that the winery co-promotes to their own list. A producer dinner where the farm shares it with their direct customers. A collaboration event where both venue and partner promote to their separate audiences.

In NZ, these partnerships are accessible in a way they might not be in a larger market. Winemakers, brewers, cheese makers, and farmers are often local and approachable. An email to a Central Otago producer asking if they'd like to be featured in a dinner and co-promote it is a conversation most of them are open to having.

**NOTE:** The marketing value of a producer partnership often exceeds the operational complexity. A winery's email list of 800 people in your city who actively like wine is a better audience for your wine dinner than almost any paid advertising channel.

## The Post-Event Capture

Every event attendee is a potential regular and a future event customer. The post-event follow-up — thanking them, mentioning what's coming next, inviting them back to the main menu — is the simplest conversion step and the one most often skipped.

A single email sent the day after your event, personalised to reference the specific evening, converts a percentage of attendees to regular customers that would otherwise never return. It takes ten minutes to write and has a material impact on your customer lifetime value numbers.
