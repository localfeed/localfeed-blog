Most NZ restaurant menus are designed aesthetically and not commercially. They look good. They do not necessarily sell the items with the best margin, or guide the diner toward the experience you want to deliver.

Menu engineering — the deliberate design of a menu to improve average spend, food cost percentage, and kitchen efficiency — is not complicated. It is systematic thinking applied to something most operators design by instinct.

## The Four-Category Framework

The classic menu engineering framework categorises every item by popularity (how often it is ordered) and profitability (how much gross profit it generates):

| | High popularity | Low popularity |
|---|---|---|
| **High profitability** | Stars — promote and maintain | Plowhorses — reposition or reprice |
| **Low profitability** | Puzzles — improve or remove | Dogs — remove |

**Stars**: Your best sellers with good margins. Feature these prominently. Do not discount them. Make sure your team recommends them.

**Plowhorses**: Popular but low margin. Either reprice them, reduce portion cost, or use them as traffic drivers that lead to higher-margin add-ons.

**Puzzles**: High potential but not selling. Look at description, positioning on the menu, and whether your floor team is recommending them.

**Dogs**: Low sales, low margin. Remove them. They complicate your kitchen prep, confuse your menu, and generate no commercial value.

## How to Run the Numbers

For every item on your menu, calculate:

1. **Food cost in dollars**: What do the ingredients for this dish cost per serve (with realistic portion size and waste factored in)?
2. **Gross profit in dollars**: Menu price minus food cost
3. **Monthly unit sales**: How many portions of this dish sell in a month?
4. **Monthly gross profit contribution**: Gross profit × monthly unit sales

Sort your full menu by monthly gross profit contribution. The top 20% of items are almost certainly your commercial foundation. The bottom 20% are candidates for removal or repricing.

## The Visual Engineering of a Menu

Where items appear on your menu influences what customers order. This is well-documented in menu psychology research and consistently confirmed in hospitality operations.

**The eye naturally scans the top right first**, then top left, then across. Premium items or high-margin items placed in these positions are more likely to be noticed and ordered.

**Box highlighting** draws attention to specific items — use it for items you want to promote, not for random selections.

**Description quality influences price tolerance**: An item described as "grass-fed Canterbury lamb, slow-braised with rosemary and served with seasonal vegetables" outsells and out-prices the same dish described as "lamb dish with vegetables."

**Remove the currency symbol**: Research consistently shows that menus without dollar signs attached to prices result in higher average spend. "26" versus "$26.00" — the latter creates friction around the spending decision.

## Menu Length and Kitchen Efficiency

Longer menus create several compounding problems:

- **Food cost**: More ingredients, more cross-utilisation complexity, more wastage as items that do not sell result in spoilage
- **Kitchen speed**: More items means slower service as the kitchen manages more simultaneous preparations
- **Quality consistency**: It is easier to execute 12 items excellently than 28 items adequately
- **Staff knowledge**: Floor staff who cannot describe and recommend the full menu default to the same three items every table

The NZ restaurants with the best customer satisfaction scores and food cost percentages are consistently running shorter menus than their less successful equivalents.

Consider removing the bottom 20–30% of your menu by sales and replacing with nothing. Test for 30 days. Your food cost will likely improve, your kitchen will run faster, and customer satisfaction will hold or improve because the items that remain are the ones you do best.

## Seasonal Menu Changes

Seasonal menu updates serve multiple commercial purposes beyond matching ingredient availability:

- **Creates newsworthy content**: A new seasonal menu is an email, an Instagram post, and a reason for regulars to come back
- **Resets customer expectations**: Customers reset their ordering habits with a new menu, which creates opportunities to shift traffic toward higher-margin items
- **Refreshes kitchen energy**: Chef and kitchen team motivation is maintained when the menu changes regularly

The optimal frequency for most NZ venues: two to four seasonal changes per year plus small interim additions and removals.

## The Online Menu Problem

Your website menu and your Google Business Profile menu should match your current in-house menu. This sounds obvious. In practice, many NZ restaurants have an outdated online menu that creates customer dissatisfaction when items they planned to order are not available.

Assign someone to update the online menu every time the in-house menu changes. The maintenance friction is worth it — customers who arrive expecting a menu that no longer exists are negative review risks.
