Most NZ venue owners think food photography requires a professional photographer, a ring light, and equipment they do not have. It does not. The food photos that drive the most Instagram engagement and Google profile clicks for NZ cafes and restaurants were taken with a phone, in natural light, by a team member who learned three things: light source, angle, and what to exclude from the frame.

## The only thing that matters more than everything else: light

Natural light is the difference between a food photo that looks like it belongs in Cuisine magazine and one that looks like a blurry crime scene. Flash kills food photography. Overhead kitchen lighting kills food photography. A phone camera pointed at a dish sitting in front of a window, between 8am and 2pm on a day with even cloud cover, will produce a better photo than most amateur setups with additional equipment.

The practical setup for a NZ cafe or restaurant:

- Identify the window in your venue that gets the most even, diffused light in the morning
- Clear a surface next to it — a table, a section of the bench, a board
- Bring the dish to the window
- Shoot from directly above (flatlay) or at a 45-degree angle to the food
- Do not use your phone's flash. Ever.

That is the setup. It costs nothing. It takes two minutes. The output is consistently better than what most NZ venues are posting.

**STAT:** 3.2x · Higher engagement rate for food photos taken in natural light versus flash or mixed artificial lighting, across NZ food Instagram accounts. Light is the variable that matters most and it is free.

## Angles that work for different types of NZ venue food

Different food types photograph best at different angles:

**Flatlay (directly overhead)**: works for cabinet spreads, boards, shared plates, pizza, breakfast dishes with multiple components. Shows the full picture and the colours equally.

**45-degree angle**: works for stacked items — burgers, toasties, tall cakes, layered desserts. Shows height and texture that a flatlay loses.

**Eye-level "hero shot"**: works for soup, coffee, single-component dishes. Creates a sense of intimacy and immediacy. Harder to get right consistently.

**Close-up/macro**: works for texture-forward subjects — a flaky pastry, a well-poured espresso, a dessert with a visible cross-section. Shows craftsmanship.

For a NZ cafe, flatlays of the cabinet spread and 45-degree shots of signature items cover 90% of your photography needs. Pick one angle per dish type and be consistent. Consistency builds a visual style that makes your feed recognisable without requiring a brand design brief.

> The flat lay of your Tuesday cabinet, shot from directly overhead in the morning light before service, takes four minutes and will be your best-performing post that week.

## What to include in the frame and what to remove

The biggest mistake in NZ venue food photography is including too much. A dirty bench. A bottle of sauce. A hand holding something without intention. A background that identifies nothing and adds noise.

The frame should contain: the food, a clean surface, possibly a prop that adds context (a coffee cup beside a pastry, a fork beside a plate, a napkin underneath a board). Nothing that requires explaining. Nothing that distracts from the dish.

Before shooting: clear the surface completely, then add back only what belongs. Less in the frame means more attention on the food.

Backgrounds that work for NZ cafe food photography:
- Bare timber table (warm, natural, versatile)
- White or off-white surface (clean, lets the food colour speak)
- Dark slate or stone (dramatic, works for evening and dessert content)
- Your actual cafe table with a bit of character (contextual, local)

Backgrounds that do not work:
- Cluttered kitchen surfaces
- Synthetic patterned tablecloths
- Surfaces with logos or branding that is not yours

**NOTE:** Before your next food photo, spend 60 seconds clearing everything from the surface and setting up with only what you intend to include. The discipline of a clear frame is more valuable than any camera upgrade.

## The three photos every NZ venue should take weekly

**The cabinet overview**: every Tuesday or whenever the cabinet resets. A flatlay of the full selection. This is the highest-value Instagram post for most NZ cafes.

**The hero dish**: whatever the kitchen is proudest of this week. The special, the seasonal item, the new addition. Shot at the angle that shows it best. This is the post that generates direct booking interest.

**The behind-the-scenes kitchen moment**: not staged, not polished. The chef plating up, the kitchen at 7am, the roast just out of the oven. This is the post that builds trust and human connection with your audience.

Three photos per week. Taken with a phone in natural light. Posted with a suburb tag and a specific caption. That is a social media strategy that most NZ venues are not executing and that most venues who execute it find transforms their Instagram performance within six weeks.
