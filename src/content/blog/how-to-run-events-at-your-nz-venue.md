Events are one of the highest-margin activities available to a NZ hospitality venue. A well-run event fills capacity at a predetermined price, removes the a la carte ordering uncertainty, allows the kitchen to plan and execute precisely, and builds the kind of community connection that regular service can't. Done badly, events are an operational nightmare with poor returns. Here's how to do them properly.

## Why Events Work Financially

The event economics are more favourable than regular service in a specific way: certainty. A Wednesday night wine dinner for 30 people at $90 per person is $2,700 in guaranteed revenue before service begins. The kitchen knows exactly what it's cooking, in what quantities, for how many people. The prep can be optimised. The service can be structured. The margin is knowable in advance.

Compare this to a regular Wednesday service: you might do 30 covers, or 12, or 45. The uncertainty costs you in over-prep, over-staffing, or under-service depending on which way it goes. Events replace that uncertainty with a number you can plan around.

> "An event night at my venue is the most relaxed service of the week. The kitchen knows exactly what it's making. The floor knows exactly how many covers. It's the easiest money we make."

**STAT:** NZ hospitality venues report that structured events — wine dinners, tasting menus, pairing nights — generate 35-50% higher revenue per seat than equivalent dine-in nights, with food cost percentages that are typically 5-8 points lower due to reduced waste and optimised purchasing.

## What Events Actually Work

The events that sell consistently in NZ hospitality share a few characteristics: they have a clear narrative (wine pairing, seasonal tasting, producer dinner), they're limited in size (scarcity drives booking), and they're priced to reflect the experience rather than just the food.

The events that don't work: too broad (a "special night" with no specific hook), too large (trying to do an event for 100 people in a space built for 60), or too cheap (priced to fill seats rather than to reflect the margin you need to make it worthwhile).

**NOTE:** Events priced too low are worse than not running events. A $45 three-course event in a venue that normally charges $45 for a main course tells your regulars that you don't value your own product. Price the event at what the experience is worth, not at what fills it fastest.

## The Promotion Timeline

Events need lead time. Four to six weeks for a mid-scale event (30-40 people). Eight to twelve weeks for a major event. The promotion timeline: announce to your email list first (the insiders get first access), then social media, then any external platforms.

The first email announcement is the most important communication. It should be personal, specific, and create genuine urgency. "We're running a Central Otago pinot dinner on June 12. Sixteen seats. $95 including matched wines. Reply to this email to reserve your spot." That's the message. It doesn't need to be elaborate.

> "The venues that sell out events consistently are the ones that have built the email list. The ones that announce events on Instagram and wonder why they don't fill are missing the fundamental tool."

**STAT:** Events promoted first to venue email lists fill an average of 40% of capacity from the initial email alone, before any public promotion. The email list is worth more for event promotion than all social media channels combined.

## Managing the Event Night

The operational discipline for an event night is different from regular service. Brief the team on the full sequence before service starts. Everyone needs to know the menu, the pacing, the key talking points for each course or wine. In a wine dinner, the floor staff need to be able to speak to the wines with enough confidence that they add to the experience rather than just pouring and retreating.

Pacing is where event nights most commonly fail. Too fast and diners feel rushed; too slow and the food is cold and people are restless. A rule of thumb: 25-30 minutes per course in a structured dinner. The kitchen and floor need to be communicating constantly on pace — one runner whose job is specifically to manage the gap between courses.

## Follow-Up: The Part Everyone Skips

The event follow-up is where you convert event attendees into regulars. An email the following day, thanking them for coming, noting which wines were featured and where to find them, with a specific invitation to a future event or a mention of the next seasonal menu. Two minutes to write. Material difference in how many of those people come back.
