Online reviews are the most influential marketing channel most NZ venues don't actively manage. They're free to respond to. They're visible to every potential customer who searches for the venue. They're increasingly the first thing a new customer checks before making a booking decision. And yet most NZ hospitality venues have a passive relationship with their reviews — checking them occasionally, responding inconsistently, and treating them as a problem to manage rather than an asset to build.

## Why Reviews Matter More Than Most Venues Think

The discovery journey for a NZ diner considering a new restaurant almost always includes a review check. The sequence: they hear about the venue (word of mouth, social media, a recommendation), they search for it on Google, and they read the reviews before deciding whether to book. The reviews are the final persuasion point before the booking decision.

A venue with 4.6 stars and 180 recent reviews is in a fundamentally different competitive position from a venue with 3.8 stars and 25 reviews from three years ago. The star rating affects click-through from search results. The review count signals activity and trust. The recency signals that the quality reflected in the reviews is current.

> "A diner who's deciding between two unfamiliar restaurants will almost always go to the one with more recent, positive reviews. Everything else being equal, reviews make the decision."

**STAT:** 73% of NZ diners check online reviews before visiting a restaurant for the first time. Venues with an average rating above 4.3 receive 2.8x more first-visit bookings from online search than venues below 4.0, even when other factors are controlled.

## How to Generate More Reviews

The gap between a venue that has 200 reviews and one that has 30 is almost never about how good the venue is. It's about how actively the venue has asked for reviews. Most diners who have a good experience don't leave a review unless they're prompted. The prompt doesn't need to be aggressive or uncomfortable — it just needs to happen.

The approaches that work: a table card with a QR code linking to your Google review page. A line in your post-visit email ("If you enjoyed your visit, we'd love a Google review — it takes two minutes and helps us enormously"). A verbal mention from front-of-house at the end of service ("We'd really appreciate your feedback online if you have a moment"). Consistent application of any of these generates a significant increase in review volume within months.

**NOTE:** Ask for reviews after the customer has had a genuinely good experience. Don't ask while they're still at the table if anything has gone slightly wrong — wait until the end of service, when the overall impression is what matters, not the one thing that wasn't perfect.

## Responding to Reviews — The Non-Optional Part

Responding to Google reviews is not optional for a NZ venue that takes its reputation seriously. Every review deserves a response — positive reviews with a brief thank-you that acknowledges something specific from the review, negative reviews with a measured, non-defensive engagement that acknowledges the concern and offers a path forward.

The review response that loses a venue business is the defensive, slightly aggressive one where the owner explains why the reviewer is wrong. Even if the reviewer is wrong, that response tells every future customer that the venue doesn't handle criticism well. The response that wins business is the one where the owner takes the criticism seriously, acknowledges it, and demonstrates that they care about getting it right.

> "The review response is not for the person who left the review. It's for the 300 people who will read both the review and the response before deciding whether to book."

**STAT:** Google research shows that 89% of consumers read business responses to reviews. Venues that respond to all reviews — positive and negative — have 12% higher review conversion rates (reviews leading to visits) than venues that respond to nothing.

## The Timing of Review Requests

The optimal time to request a review is within 24 hours of the visit, when the experience is fresh and the positive feeling from a good meal is still present. An email the following day — "Thank you for visiting last night. We'd love to know what you thought" — with a direct link to the Google review page converts significantly better than a request made at the table or a follow-up email a week later.

## Platforms Beyond Google

Google is the dominant review platform for NZ restaurants, but it's not the only one. TripAdvisor matters for venues with significant tourist traffic. Facebook reviews matter for venues whose primary audience uses Facebook. For cafes specifically, Instagram can function as a de facto review platform through tagged posts and stories.

The strategy: prioritise Google (highest search integration, most discovery impact), manage TripAdvisor if tourist traffic is significant, and monitor Facebook. Don't try to be everywhere at once — focus on the platforms your actual customer demographic uses.
