# Best Restaurants in Wellington 2026

Wellington does not have Auckland's population or Sydney's glamour. What it has is a food culture per square kilometre that is unmatched in the Southern Hemisphere. A city of 215,000 people that supports more genuinely excellent restaurants than cities three times its size, fed by a dining public that is unusually knowledgeable, unusually loyal, and unusually unforgiving about quality.

The explanation is partly structural — Wellington's compact geography creates a walkable city where restaurants live and die on neighbourhood repeat business rather than tourist throughput. Partly cultural — the public sector and university population creates a class of diner who reads, travels, and thinks about food. Partly competitive — the density means every restaurant is five minutes from three competitors, and there is nowhere to hide.

This guide covers the Wellington restaurants worth crossing the city for — or crossing Cook Strait for.

---

## Logan Brown

**What it is:** Wellington's most important restaurant. Going since 1996 on Cuba Street in a converted bank. The room against which all other Wellington fine dining is measured.

**Why it matters:** Logan Brown has held its position at the summit of Wellington dining for three decades not through complacency but through continuous excellence. Steve Logan's vision — serious food in a genuinely warm room, grounded in New Zealand produce, without the hauteur that fine dining once required — shaped what Wellington dining became. The wine list is one of the best in the country. The cooking is still among the best in the city.

**What to order:** The tasting menu is the full expression. À la carte: the seafood (Cook Strait access is reflected in the kitchen), the lamb, and whatever the kitchen is particularly proud of this week — ask.

**Price range:** $$$$. Wellington's best fine dining at Wellington's highest prices. Worth it.

**Book ahead:** Always. Weeks in advance for Saturday dinner.

## Shepherd

**What it is:** Tory Street farm-to-table — the most ingredient-focused restaurant in Wellington, with a kitchen that changes its menu based on what is actually ready rather than what is convenient.

**Why it matters:** Shepherd has built something that is increasingly rare: a restaurant with a genuine point of view that it holds to without compromise. Named farms. Seasonal menus that actually change with the season. A wine list curated around the same philosophy. The lamb is exceptional (the name is not accidental). The vegetable dishes are worth ordering even if you did not think you were ordering vegetables tonight.

**What to order:** Follow the kitchen. Whatever is leading the menu this week is leading it for a reason. The bread service. The natural wine pairings the floor team suggest.

**Price range:** $$$–$$$$. Worth it for the quality and the coherence of what you are eating.

---

## Ortega Fish Shack

**What it is:** Wellington's finest seafood restaurant. On Majoribanks Street, slightly off the main Te Aro cluster, worth finding.

**Why it matters:** Wellington is a harbour city on the edge of Cook Strait, with some of the best seafood in the Southern Hemisphere accessible to its kitchens. Ortega has built its entire identity around that fact. The cooking is European — Spanish and French influences — applied to product that is as good as any in New Zealand. The bouillabaisse, the whole roasted fish, the shellfish preparations: these are the things Wellington food people come back for.

**What to order:** The daily specials are the honest guide to what the kitchen is doing best. Bluff oysters when in season. The bouillabaisse when it is on. The crudo. Whatever whole fish arrived that morning.

**Price range:** $$$. The product quality justifies it.

## Charley Noble

**What it is:** Waterfront dining in the Frank Kitts Park area — one of Wellington's best harbour-view dining rooms, with food that holds up independent of the position.

**Why it matters:** Charley Noble has managed the difficult balancing act of being a destination waterfront restaurant that is also a good restaurant. The menu runs from serious shared plates to main event dishes, with a wine programme that pays attention to New Zealand's best small producers. The harbour view in Wellington's particular dramatic weather — the kind that only Wellington gets — is a genuine experience.

**What to order:** The shared plates format works well here. The fish is always the right choice. The beef cuts from the grill. The natural wine section of the list.

**Price range:** $$$. Waterfront premium applied moderately.

**The room:** Harbour views, the waterfront promenade, Wellington's best outdoor dining position on a clear day.

## Il Casino

**What it is:** Thorndon Italian — a Wellington institution that has been feeding the ministerial and diplomatic crowd since the late 1980s.

**Why it matters:** Il Casino operates in the old-school Italian restaurant tradition — proper pasta, proper technique, a wine list that takes the Italian section seriously — without the dated atmosphere that description might suggest. The room is elegant. The service is formal without being stiff. The pasta is made in-house and it is the reason to go.

**What to order:** The handmade pasta, whatever preparation is on today. The osso buco. The tiramisu.

**Price range:** $$$–$$$$. Thorndon pricing — conservative and justified.

**The room:** Elegant, quiet, the right restaurant for a serious business dinner.

---

## Boulcott Street Bistro

**What it is:** A Victorian cottage turned Wellington CBD restaurant — one of the most distinctive rooms in the city, and a kitchen that has earned the room.

**Why it matters:** The Boulcott Street Bistro setting is genuinely singular — a two-storey Victorian wooden cottage in the middle of the CBD, surrounded by modern buildings, with a garden that functions as a terrace in summer. The food inside is a classic bistro menu executed seriously: French influences, New Zealand produce, a wine list with depth and intelligence.

**What to order:** The duck. The beef cuts. The seasonal fish. The desserts, which are better than bistro desserts usually are.

**Price range:** $$$. One of the better-value serious restaurants in the CBD.

**The room:** The cottage itself is the reason to visit. The upstairs dining room has a particular warmth that Wellington's newer restaurants have not replicated.

---

## Zibibbo

**What it is:** A Wellington fixture on Post Office Square — Mediterranean in orientation, relaxed in format, consistently good.

**Why it matters:** Zibibbo has been feeding Wellington for long enough to have earned its place in the city's dining geography without becoming complacent. The mezze format and the Mediterranean range — North African, Middle Eastern, and Southern European dishes alongside each other — suits Wellington's cosmopolitan dining public. The outdoor terrace in summer is one of the CBD's best positions.

**What to order:** Build a spread of mezze. The lamb dishes. The brunch, which is excellent and worth the weekday visit.

**Price range:** $$–$$$. Among the better value options in the CBD for the quality delivered.

## How to Eat Wellington

**Walk between everything.** Wellington's dining geography is compact — Logan Brown to Shepherd to Ortega to Loretta is a manageable walk. The city rewards pedestrian dining in a way that Auckland and Christchurch cannot.

**The weather is part of the experience.** Wellington's famous wind and sideways rain are not bugs — they make the transition from a chilly walk to a warm, lit restaurant one of the city's reliable pleasures. Embrace it.

**Book the top three well ahead.** Logan Brown, Shepherd, and Ortega Fish Shack fill for Friday and Saturday dinner. Book ahead, or eat midweek.

**Midweek Wellington is genuinely excellent.** The public service machinery keeps the city populated and restaurant-going through the week. Tuesday through Thursday in Wellington produces great dining at lower demand — the same kitchens, more relaxed rooms.

Several Wellington venues list their own midweek specials and quiet-night offers on [LocalFeed](https://localfeed.app) — not forced platform discounts, but offers the restaurants design themselves for the nights they want to fill. It is how the city's food community eats well without always paying peak prices.
