# Best Restaurants in Te Aro, Wellington 2026

Te Aro is Wellington's beating heart. Not the postcard Wellington of the waterfront and the beehive — the real one. Cuba Street, Tory Street, Dixon Street, the lanes between. This is where Wellington's food culture has concentrated for thirty years, and where it keeps reinventing itself without losing what made it good.

The suburb runs on foot traffic, on regulars, on a dining public that is unusually informed about what it is eating and where it comes from. You do not survive in Te Aro by being mediocre. The competition is too close and the customers too opinionated.

These are the restaurants worth planning your visit around.

---

## Logan Brown

**What it is:** Wellington's most important fine dining institution. Cuba Street, going since 1996. A converted bank building that became the room against which every other Wellington fine dining room is measured.

**Why it matters:** Steve Logan and Al Brown's original vision — serious cuisine in a non-serious atmosphere, with a commitment to New Zealand produce that was pioneering in the 1990s and still honest now — has outlasted dozens of attempts to replicate it. The banking chamber setting, with its soaring ceiling and booth seating, is genuinely spectacular. The food underneath it is worth the room.

**What to order:** The tasting menu is the right call for a significant occasion. À la carte, the seafood is consistently exceptional — Wellington's access to Cook Strait fish and shellfish is reflected in what arrives at this kitchen. The wine list is one of the best in the country.

**Price range:** $$$$. Fine dining. Worth it for the right evening.

**Book ahead:** Always. This is not a walk-in restaurant for dinner.

## Shepherd

**What it is:** One of Wellington's most interesting restaurants — farm-to-table done with genuine rigour, housed in a corner site on Tory Street that has become a destination.

**Why it matters:** Shepherd has built its menu around named farms and seasonal availability in a way that reads as conviction rather than marketing. The approach produces a menu that changes constantly and sometimes produces the most interesting plate you will eat in Wellington that month. The wine list is natural-forward and well curated.

**What to order:** Follow the kitchen's lead. The lamb is usually exceptional — the name is not ironic, and the sourcing is serious. Vegetable dishes here are worth ordering on their own terms, not as a concession.

**Price range:** $$$–$$$$. Positioned as a special occasion restaurant for Wellington, but the weeknight atmosphere is more relaxed than the price point suggests.

**The room:** Warm. Good acoustics for a Wellington restaurant, which matters more than it should.

---

## Loretta

**What it is:** A Cuba Street neighbourhood bistro that does everything it does at a higher level than you expect from a casual room.

**Why it matters:** Loretta fills a gap that many hospitality precincts lack: a genuinely good restaurant you can eat at four times a year without the weight of a special occasion. The brunch is justifiably famous. The dinner, which does not always get the same attention, is equally serious.

**What to order:** The pasta is good. The brunch boards are worth the wait (and there will be a wait on weekends). At dinner, the seasonal vegetable dishes often outperform the obvious choices on the menu.

**Price range:** $$–$$$. One of the better-value serious restaurants in Te Aro.

**The room:** Busy. Convivial. Loud on weekends in the best way.

---

## Ortega Fish Shack

**What it is:** Wellington's best seafood restaurant. Not what you picture from "fish shack" — this is a serious restaurant with a serious wine list and exceptional product knowledge.

**Why it matters:** Wellington is a harbour city with some of the best seafood in New Zealand available locally. Ortega has built an institution around that fact. The cooking is European in technique — Spanish and French influences — applied to fish and shellfish from the Cook Strait and beyond. The mussel and clam preparations have their own dedicated following.

**What to order:** Ask what came in today. The daily specials are the most honest guide to what the kitchen does best. The bouillabaisse when it is on. The crudo. The Bluff oysters when in season.

**Price range:** $$$. Appropriate for the quality of the product.

**The room:** A genuine Majoribanks Street institution. Book ahead.

---

## Matterhorn

**What it is:** A Te Aro original — Cuba Street bar and restaurant going back to the 1960s, reinvented multiple times without losing its cultural position.

**Why it matters:** Matterhorn is one of the few Wellington venues that functions as both a serious restaurant and a serious bar simultaneously, at different hours. The food is consistently better than bars have to be. The cocktail programme is genuinely good. The room on a Friday night is one of Wellington's best experiences — the energy without the chaos.

**What to order:** The bar food is surprisingly serious. At dinner, the grill section delivers. The cocktail list is worth exploring.

**Price range:** $$–$$$. Food in the middle of the Te Aro range.

**The room:** Dark, booths, the long bar. Architecture that has been photographed many times for a reason.

---

## Capitol

**What it is:** Kent Terrace fine dining — a converted cinema that became one of Wellington's most reliable high-end dining rooms.

**Why it matters:** Capitol operates slightly outside the Te Aro cluster (Kent Terrace, technically) but draws the same dining public. The brasserie format — European in structure, seasonal in execution — produces food that holds up at the price. The wine list has genuine depth in the New Zealand section.

**What to order:** The beef is consistently excellent. The seafood specials. The cheese selection for dessert, which is more interesting than most Wellington restaurants manage.

**Price range:** $$$$. Fine dining pricing, with the experience to justify it.

---

## Five Boroughs

**What it is:** New York-style diner done seriously, on Tory Street, that has become a Wellington institution for exactly the food it promises.

**Why it matters:** Five Boroughs occupies a specific niche — the place you go when you want a genuinely good burger, a stack of American pancakes at brunch, or a Reuben that is actually worth eating — and it occupies it with more quality than the category requires. This is not lowbrow food treated as novelty. It is lowbrow food executed correctly.

**What to order:** The cheeseburger. The pastrami. The breakfast at any hour. The milkshake, which is better than it needs to be.

**Price range:** $$. The most honest value on Tory Street.

**The room:** Counter seating, booth seating, a room that looks like it arrived from somewhere else and has been here forever.

## How to Eat in Te Aro

**Walk the blocks.** Te Aro is dense. Shepherd, Ortega, Five Boroughs, Loretta, and a dozen other good venues are within a ten-minute walk of each other. The dining culture here is pedestrian — park once and wander.

**Book for the serious ones.** Logan Brown, Shepherd, Ortega Fish Shack — all need advance booking for weekend dinners. Midweek is significantly easier and the rooms are more relaxed.

**Midweek deals exist.** Several Te Aro venues list their own midweek specials on [LocalFeed](https://localfeed.app) — not platform-imposed discounts, but offers the venues design themselves. It is a legitimate way to get into Wellington's better restaurants at prices that make weeknight dining feel less like a commitment.
