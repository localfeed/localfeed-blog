# Best Restaurants in Queenstown 2026

Queenstown has a food reputation it has had to fight for. The city processes three million visitors a year and the economics of tourism dining — volume, speed, captive audience, price tolerance — create natural pressure toward mediocrity. The venison pies and the lakefront photo opportunities are everywhere.

But underneath the tourist infrastructure, Queenstown has developed a genuine dining scene. A small number of restaurants have chosen to be taken seriously rather than to be convenient, and they have attracted a kitchen culture that produces food worth travelling for independent of the view.

These are the ones that matter.

---

## Rata

**What it is:** Josh Emett's flagship restaurant in the Wood Lane precinct — sophisticated New Zealand cooking from one of the country's most decorated chefs.

**Why it matters:** Rata arrived in Queenstown with credentials (Emett trained under Gordon Ramsay, ran Maze in New York) and has justified them. The menu is rooted in New Zealand produce — Central Otago venison, Canterbury lamb, local game — executed with a precision that Queenstown's dining scene did not reliably have before it. The room is one of the most beautiful in New Zealand hospitality.

**What to order:** The Central Otago lamb. The venison, which arrives in various preparations and is consistently the kitchen's strongest statement. The bread service. The tasting menu for a significant occasion.

**Price range:** $$$$. Premium Queenstown — not cheap, but the food and room justify the spend.

**Book ahead:** Essential. Rata on a weekend in high season is booked days or weeks out.

---

## Amisfield Winery & Bistro

**What it is:** A Lake Hayes winery restaurant that has become one of the definitive New Zealand wine-dining experiences — twenty minutes from Queenstown, worth every minute of the drive.

**Why it matters:** Amisfield's food-and-wine integration is genuine rather than gestural. The kitchen designs around the estate's Pinot Noir and Riesling in a way that most winery restaurants talk about but do not actually achieve. The "Trust the Chef" menu — no choices, the kitchen sends what is ready, paired with the winery's range — is one of the best dining experiences in the South Island.

**What to order:** The Trust the Chef menu with wine pairing. This is the correct way to experience Amisfield. If à la carte, the rabbit, the duck, or whatever the kitchen is featuring from the Central Otago estate.

**Price range:** $$$$. Winery restaurant pricing — plan for the drive and the meal as the event.

**The setting:** Lake Hayes vineyard. The lunch service in summer on the terrace overlooking the vines is a specific New Zealand pleasure.

---

## The Bunker

**What it is:** Underground Queenstown fine dining — literally, a restaurant accessed through a door off The Mall that descends into a warm, log-fired room that feels like a private club.

**Why it matters:** The Bunker has solved Queenstown's atmosphere problem — the town's restaurant dining can feel transactional, built for throughput. The Bunker is the opposite: intimate, slightly secret, the kind of room that creates the impression you have found something most visitors do not find. The food is a serious match for the room.

**What to order:** The venison. The wagyu. The seafood — Queenstown's access to Bluff oysters and Southern Ocean fish is reflected here. The cocktail list is seriously good and the bar team knows it.

**Price range:** $$$$. Fine dining in a format that feels more like a club than a restaurant. Worth it.

**The room:** You descend stairs off The Mall. Log fire, leather armchairs, the kind of lighting that makes everything look better. Book a table in advance and arrive for a drink at the bar first.

---

## Botswana Butchery

**What it is:** Waterfront dining on the Queenstown lakefront — a grill restaurant with views across Lake Wakatipu that no other Auckland or Wellington restaurant can offer.

**Why it matters:** Botswana Butchery manages the difficult balance of being a legitimately good restaurant in a location that could coast on its view. The meat programme is serious — prime cuts from trusted New Zealand producers, executed by a kitchen that knows it is competing with the scenery and compensates by not cutting corners. The wine list is extensive and the floor team can navigate it.

**What to order:** The prime aged beef. The Wagyu cuts. The seafood, which arrives from the southern coast. The cheese selection. The view from the terrace at sunset, which is not something you order but should not miss.

**Price range:** $$$$. Premium lakefront positioning — the view is priced in, but the food earns its keep.

## Rata: The Wood Lane Context

The Wood Lane precinct (Rata, Fishbone, and a cluster of adjacent venues) has become Queenstown's serious dining district. If you are building an evening, this is the right neighbourhood — quieter than The Mall strip, more serious in ambition, within walking distance of the town centre.

---

## Fishbone Bar & Grill

**What it is:** One of Queenstown's best-value serious restaurants — a bar and grill that does what it does at a higher level than the tourists who fill it might expect.

**Why it matters:** Fishbone has been on Beach Street long enough to have earned its reputation. The seafood is the point — Bluff oysters, southern scallops, locally sourced fish — and the kitchen treats it with the respect it deserves. The bar is genuinely good and the atmosphere is the right kind of buzzy.

**What to order:** The Bluff oysters when in season. The fish of the day. The crayfish when it is on. The whitebait fritters. The wine list has strong Central Otago representation.

**Price range:** $$$. Better value than the lakefront venues for comparable product quality.

---

## Fergburger

**What it is:** The most famous burger in New Zealand — possibly the most famous burger in the Southern Hemisphere. Fergburger on Shotover Street.

**Why it matters:** Listing Fergburger in a guide to Queenstown's best restaurants is not sycophancy — it is accurate. The queues are real (midnight queues on summer nights are a genuine Queenstown experience), but the product behind the queues is actually exceptional. Fresh-baked buns, quality beef, generous builds. If you are in Queenstown and have not been, go once. You will understand the queue.

**What to order:** The Big Al (double patty, the standard). The Bun Laden if you are very hungry. Go at an off-peak time if you dislike queuing.

**Price range:** $. The most democratic meal in Queenstown.

## Eating in Queenstown: What to Know

**Book the top rooms well ahead.** Rata, Amisfield, and The Bunker fill quickly in high season (December–February, July ski season). Book before you arrive.

**The lakefront view costs more.** Botswana Butchery and Soul Kitchen have premium positions. The food is worth it, but the view is priced in — factor this into your expectations.

**Amisfield requires transport.** Twenty minutes from Queenstown toward Arrowtown. Worth it. Drive (designate a driver), take a taxi, or arrange it as part of the experience. Do not skip it because of the logistics.

**Midweek dining in the shoulder season is exceptional.** Queenstown in April or October — after the summer tourists, before the ski season — is the city at its best for dining. Less competition for tables, more attention from the floor, the same mountain backdrop.
