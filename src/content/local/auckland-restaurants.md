# Best Restaurants in Auckland 2026

Auckland is New Zealand's dining capital by population and by diversity. A city of 1.7 million, home to the largest Polynesian urban population in the world, with an Asian-origin population that constitutes nearly thirty percent of the city — the food geography that results is unlike anywhere else in Australasia.

The serious restaurant scene sits alongside the best immigrant cooking in the Southern Hemisphere. The same city that has Cocoro (Auckland's finest Japanese restaurant) has the Otara Food and Cultural Market on Saturday morning. Both are worth your time. Both are best in class.

This guide covers the restaurants worth planning a visit around — across Auckland's multiple dining precincts.

---

## The Precincts

Auckland's dining is not one place. It is a cluster of neighbourhoods, each with its own character, each worth understanding before choosing where to eat.

**Ponsonby/Grey Lynn** — Auckland's most celebrated food strip. Ponsonby Road and its surrounding streets carry the highest concentration of excellent restaurants in the country. Orphans Kitchen, Gemmayze Street, Azabu, Cocoro, Prego. Also: Apero, the natural wine bar, and a cafe scene that set Auckland's standard for twenty years.

**Auckland CBD (Britomart/Viaduct)** — the city centre has matured. The Grove (fine dining), Depot (Al Brown's raw bar institution), Ebisu (serious Japanese), Baduzzi (Northern Italian), Clooney (tasting menu). Accessible by train from anywhere on the network.

**Parnell/Newmarket** — Auckland's east-side dining. Older in style, reliable in quality. Cibo, Non Solo Pizza, Antoine's. The area has not been as discussed as Ponsonby but has been quietly excellent for decades.

**Takapuna (North Shore)** — across the Harbour Bridge, a dining scene that no longer requires an apology. Ika for seafood, Estuary for occasions.

## The Best Restaurants in Auckland, by Category

### Fine Dining

**Cocoro** (Ponsonby) — Auckland's finest Japanese restaurant. Makoto Tokuyama's kaiseki-influenced cooking is world-class by any standard. Omakase, or the set menu. Book weeks ahead.

**The Grove** (CBD, St Patrick's Square) — the most ambitious fine dining in the city. Tasting menu format, exceptional execution. Book ahead.

**Clooney** (CBD, Federal Street) — tasting menu with wine pairing. The CBD's serious alternative to The Grove. A smaller room and a kitchen that takes its commitments seriously.

### Modern New Zealand

**Orphans Kitchen** (Ponsonby) — ethical sourcing, whole-animal cookery, farm-to-table before it was marketing. The bread. The constantly rotating menu. Mains $38–$48.

**The Engine Room** (Northcote) — one of Auckland's most important neighbourhood restaurants. North Shore, worth the drive. The cooking reflects a kitchen that does not compromise.

### International, Genuine

**Gemmayze Street** (Ponsonby) — Auckland's best Lebanese. Real mezze, real technique, real point of view. The kibbeh nayyeh. The hummus that surprises you. Book ahead for weekends.

**Azabu** (Ponsonby Central) — Japanese-Peruvian fusion done at a level that justifies the category. The robata grill. The tiradito. Cocktails worth ordering.

**Ebisu** (Britomart) — serious Japanese izakaya. Product integrity, good sourcing, a sake and cocktail list that the floor team knows well.

**Baduzzi** (North Wharf) — Northern Italian, Venetian-influenced. The meatballs. The hand-rolled pasta. The Italian wine list.

### Neighbourhood Excellence

**Prego** (Ponsonby) — Italian institution, 1989. Still the best room on the strip for a Friday night. The handmade pasta. The seafood.

**Depot** (CBD, Federal Street) — Al Brown's raw bar. No reservations, counter seating, the best no-reservations option in Auckland. The fried chicken sliders. The oysters. The clam chowder.

**Non Solo Pizza** (Parnell) — handmade pasta, wood-fired pizza, Italian wine list. Decades old, still completely reliable.

## Auckland's Other Food Story: The Markets and the Ethnic Dining

Auckland's immigrant communities produce some of the best food in the city, almost entirely outside the restaurant guide economy.

**Otara Food and Cultural Market** (Saturday morning, Otara) — the largest Pacific market in New Zealand. Hangi, umu, Samoan chop suey, Tongan otai, Fijian curry. A forty-five minute experience that gives you more insight into Auckland's food culture than most restaurant dinners.

**Avondale Sunday Market** — Chinese, Southeast Asian, and Pacific cooking. Fresh produce and prepared food from the communities that have made West Auckland into one of the most interesting food neighbourhoods in the country.

**Dominion Road** (Mt Roskill to Balmoral) — Auckland's best-known restaurant strip for Chinese cuisine. The dumpling houses, the BBQ shops, the hand-pulled noodle operations. A different Auckland from Ponsonby and equally important.

## When and How to Eat Well in Auckland

**Book in advance for the serious rooms.** Cocoro, The Grove, Orphans Kitchen, Gemmayze Street — all need advance booking for weekend dinner. Cocoro is often booked weeks ahead. Plan before you arrive.

**Ponsonby midweek is underrated.** The same restaurants, half the noise. Tuesday to Thursday on Ponsonby Road is when Auckland food people eat — not Saturday.

**The CBD has improved significantly.** The assumption that the best food is in the suburbs is outdated. Britomart has matured and the CBD is now a genuine first choice rather than a compromise.

**Eat at the markets.** The Otara and Avondale markets are not tourist activities — they are where Auckland's real food story is visible, and they are worth attending for the food alone.

Several Auckland venues across multiple suburbs list their own midweek offers on [LocalFeed](https://localfeed.app) — not platform-forced discounts, but the offers the restaurants design themselves for the nights they want to fill. It is a legitimate way to eat at Auckland's better restaurants on their quietest nights.
